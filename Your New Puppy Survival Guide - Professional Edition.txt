                            INTRODUCTION
                          Before You Begin

Congratulations. You have a puppy.

You probably already know that your life is about to change. The
early mornings, the accidents on the carpet, the tiny teeth marks
on your favourite shoes—they're all coming. But so is the joy, the
companionship, and the unconditional love that only a dog can
bring.

Here's something I wish someone had told me earlier: dogs are
not complicated. Humans make them complicated. We project our
emotions onto them. We treat them like furry children. We get
frustrated when they don't understand our words. And then we
wonder why training feels so hard.

The truth is simpler than you think.

Dogs want structure. They want to know what's expected. They want
calm, confident guidance. And when they get it, they relax. They
listen. They become the companion you always imagined.

This book exists because I believe training should be simple,
kind, and effective. You don't need complicated theories or harsh
methods. You need a clear path forward and the confidence to
follow it.


                              ───

                         WHAT THIS BOOK IS

This is a practical guide. Every chapter gives you specific steps
you can take today. No fluff, no filler—just what works.

The training philosophy here blends two approaches that some
people think are opposites:

     1. Positive reinforcement—reward what you want to see more of
     2. Calm, assertive leadership—provide structure and clarity

These are not contradictions. Dogs need both. They need to know
that good behaviour leads to good things. And they need to feel
that someone is calmly in charge. Without rewards, training feels
like punishment. Without structure, freedom feels like chaos.

The guiding principle is simple:

     Use the least harsh method that is likely to work.

That means we focus on setting your puppy up for success rather
than waiting for them to fail. We reward the behaviours we want
rather than punishing the ones we don't. And we build trust rather
than fear.

But it also means we don't let chaos reign. We set boundaries.
We follow through. We are consistent, every single time. This
isn't about being "strict"—it's about being clear. And clarity
is kindness.

                              ───

                       WHAT THIS BOOK IS NOT

This book is not a replacement for professional help if your dog
has serious behavioural issues like aggression, severe anxiety, or
trauma. Those situations require hands-on guidance from a
qualified, force-free trainer.

This book is also not a promise of perfection. Your puppy will
make mistakes. You will make mistakes. That's not failure—that's
learning.

                              ───

                       A NOTE ON CHOOSING THE RIGHT DOG

If you haven't gotten your puppy yet, take a moment to think
about fit. This decision will shape the next 10-15 years of
your life.

Consider honestly:

     •  Your lifestyle—how much time and energy do you have?
     •  Your living space—apartment or house with a yard?
     •  Your activity level—couch potato or marathon runner?
     •  Your experience—first dog or experienced handler?
     •  Your patience level—can you handle a challenging breed?

UNDERSTANDING ENERGY LEVELS:

Dogs are born with a certain energy level. You can manage it,
channel it, and help them learn to regulate it—but you cannot
fundamentally change it. A high-energy dog will always need
significant daily exercise and mental stimulation. A low-energy
dog will be content with less.

Problems arise when there's a mismatch. A Border Collie in a
small flat with an owner who works long hours is a recipe for
frustration. A Basset Hound with a marathon runner might be
equally mismatched.

Here's a rough guide:

     HIGH ENERGY: Border Collies, Belgian Malinois, Jack Russells,
     Australian Shepherds, Huskies, Vizslas, Weimaraners

     MEDIUM ENERGY: Labradors, Golden Retrievers, Beagles, Boxers,
     Cocker Spaniels, Standard Poodles

     LOWER ENERGY: Bulldogs, Basset Hounds, Cavalier King Charles,
     Shih Tzus, Great Danes (despite size), Greyhounds (surprisingly)

But remember: every dog is an individual. Breed gives you a
starting point, not a guarantee.

INDEPENDENT VS. PEOPLE-FOCUSED:

Some breeds were developed to work closely with humans—think
retrievers, herding dogs, and spaniels. These dogs often want
to please and may be easier to train.

Other breeds were developed to work independently—think hounds,
terriers, and many guardian breeds. These dogs think for
themselves. Training requires more patience and creativity.

Neither is "better." But knowing what you're working with helps
you set realistic expectations.

There's no "best" breed—only the best fit for YOU.

If you adopt from a shelter, ask about the dog's history and
temperament. Meet them in a calm setting. See how they respond
to you. Watch their energy level, not just their appearance.

If you get a puppy from a breeder, choose one who prioritises
health and temperament over looks. Meet the parents if possible.
The mother's temperament often predicts the puppies'. Ask
questions about the litter's early socialisation.

A well-matched dog makes training easier. A mismatched dog can
be frustrating for both of you—and unfair to the dog.


                              ───

                  IF YOU'RE CHOOSING A PUPPY

If you're still in the process of selecting a puppy, here's what
matters most:

FROM A BREEDER:

     •  NEVER buy a puppy under 8 weeks old. Puppies need at
        least 8 weeks with their mother and littermates to learn
        crucial social skills, bite inhibition, and emotional
        regulation.
     •  Meet the mother if possible. Her temperament often
        predicts the puppies'.
     •  Ask what early socialisation they've done.
     •  A good breeder asks YOU questions—about your lifestyle,
        experience, and home. If they'll sell to anyone, be wary.
     •  Avoid puppy mills and pet shop puppies. Dogs from
        factories often carry behavioural problems that are
        difficult or impossible to resolve.

FROM A SHELTER OR RESCUE:

     •  Ask about the dog's history and any known behaviours.
     •  Spend time with them in a calm environment before deciding.
     •  Understand that rescue dogs may come with baggage, but
        they can become wonderful companions with patience.
     •  Some rescues specialise in puppies; others focus on adults.

MEETING THE PUPPY:

When you meet potential puppies, observe:

     •  Is the puppy curious and approaching you (good sign)?
     •  Or hiding and fearful (may indicate poor socialisation)?
     •  Is the puppy extremely hyperactive, or able to settle?
     •  How do they interact with their littermates?

Neither the boldest nor the shyest puppy in a litter is necessarily
the best choice. Look for puppies who are curious, recover quickly
from surprise, and show interest in people.

THE FIRST EIGHT WEEKS MATTER

A puppy's first 8 weeks with their mother teach them:

     •  How hard is too hard when biting (bite inhibition)
     •  How to read other dogs' signals
     •  How to calm themselves down
     •  Basic canine social rules

Puppies removed too early often struggle with these skills. If
you're given a puppy younger than 8 weeks, understand you'll need
to work harder on bite inhibition and canine social skills.


                              ───

If you already have your puppy, don't worry—this book will help
you regardless of breed or background. But understanding your
dog's natural tendencies will help you train smarter.

                              ───

                         HOW TO USE THIS BOOK

The book is organised in seven parts, following the natural journey
of raising a puppy:

     Part One:    Understanding how your puppy thinks
     Part Two:    The foundations that prevent problems
     Part Three:  Building communication and basic skills
     Part Four:   Handling common challenges
     Part Five:   Taking training into the real world
     Part Six:    Fun, games, and tricks
     Part Seven:  The long-term view

You can read straight through, or jump to the chapter you need
most right now. If your puppy is having accidents in the house,
start with Chapter 4. If biting is driving you crazy, go to
Chapter 12. The book is designed to meet you where you are.

                              ───

                          A FINAL NOTE

Your puppy is not trying to be difficult. They are not stubborn,
spiteful, or stupid. They are simply a baby animal learning how
to exist in a human world.

Your job is not to dominate them. Your job is to guide them.

If you remember nothing else from this book, remember this:

     Your puppy is always doing the best they can
     with the skills they currently have.

     Your job is to help them build more skills.

Let's begin.

                    UNDERSTANDING YOUR PUPPY

                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 1

              The Big Picture: How Puppies Really Learn
                    ─────────────────────────────

If you understand this chapter, everything else in this book will
make more sense. You'll also be much kinder to yourself—and to
your puppy.

                              ───

                  YOUR DOG IS AN ANIMAL FIRST

Before we dive into training techniques, we need to start with
a fundamental truth:

Your dog is an animal first.

Not a baby. Not a child. Not a furry human. An animal.

This isn't cold or unloving—it's respectful. When we treat dogs
like humans, we often fail to meet their actual needs. We give
them what WE would want, not what THEY need.

Dogs need:

     •  Physical exercise
     •  Mental stimulation
     •  Clear structure and rules
     •  Calm, confident leadership

When these needs are met, behaviour problems often disappear on
their own. When they're not met, no amount of training will fix
the underlying frustration.

Love your dog deeply. But love them as a dog.

                              ───

                  CALM-ASSERTIVE ENERGY

Dogs are experts at reading energy. They sense your emotional
state before you even speak.

If you're anxious, frustrated, or uncertain, your dog feels it.
If you're calm, confident, and clear, your dog feels that too.

CALM-ASSERTIVE ENERGY means:

     •  You are relaxed but in control
     •  You know what you want
     •  You're not angry, rushed, or nervous
     •  You project quiet confidence

This energy tells your dog: "I've got this. You can relax."

Compare these two scenarios:

     NERVOUS OWNER: "Oh no, there's another dog! What if he
     barks? Please don't pull, please don't pull..."
     (Tight leash, tense body, high-pitched voice)

     CALM OWNER: *Sees other dog* *Keeps walking at same pace*
     *Relaxed leash, steady breathing, no big reaction*

Same situation. Completely different energy. The dog reads both.

Practise projecting calm confidence, especially in challenging
moments. Your dog is watching.

                              ───

                  DOGS DON'T SPEAK HUMAN—THEY READ PATTERNS

Puppies don't understand words at first. They don't know what
"sit" means or why you're upset when they chew the couch. What
they do understand is patterns.

They notice what happens right after they do something. They
observe how people move, where the good things appear, and which
places feel safe or scary.

Every time your puppy does something, they're asking a simple
question:

     "Did that make my life better or worse?"

Your job is to answer that question on purpose—not by accident.

If jumping on you results in attention, jumping increases.
If sitting calmly results in treats, sitting increases.
If pulling on the leash gets them where they want to go, pulling
increases.

This isn't stubbornness. It's logic.


                              ───

                  READING YOUR DOG'S BODY LANGUAGE

Dogs communicate constantly—just not with words. Learning to
read their body language is one of the most valuable skills you
can develop.

RELAXED AND CONTENT:

     •  Loose, wiggly body
     •  Soft, relaxed ears (position varies by breed)
     •  Open mouth, "smiling" expression
     •  Tail in neutral position, may wag slowly
     •  Weight evenly distributed

ALERT AND INTERESTED:

     •  Ears forward, focused
     •  Closed mouth, intent gaze
     •  Body weight slightly forward
     •  Tail up (but not stiff)
     •  May freeze briefly before reacting

STRESSED OR ANXIOUS:

     •  Whale eye (whites of eyes showing)
     •  Lip licking (not around food)
     •  Yawning (when not tired)
     •  Panting (when not hot or exercised)
     •  Turning head or body away
     •  Low body posture
     •  Tail tucked
     •  Ears pinned back
     •  Pacing or restlessness

FEARFUL:

     •  Body low or crouched
     •  Tail tucked tightly
     •  Ears flat against head
     •  May try to escape or hide
     •  Trembling
     •  May freeze completely
     •  May urinate submissively

PLAYFUL:

     •  Play bow (front end down, rear up)
     •  Bouncy movements
     •  Exaggerated facial expressions
     •  May bark in short bursts
     •  Will pause and restart to show it's play

AGGRESSIVE OR THREATENING (WARNING SIGNS):

     •  Hard, fixed stare
     •  Stiff body
     •  Weight forward on front legs
     •  Raised hackles (hair along spine)
     •  Showing teeth
     •  Growling or snarling
     •  Tail high and stiff (or tucked if fear-based)

IMPORTANT: Growling is communication. A dog who growls is
TELLING you they're uncomfortable. Never punish growling—you'll
remove the warning without fixing the feeling.

When you learn to read these signals, you can intervene before
problems escalate. You'll know when your puppy is overwhelmed
and needs a break. You'll see stress before it becomes a bite.

Watch your dog. They're always talking to you.


                              ───

                  THE GOLDEN RULE OF THIS BOOK

Here is the principle that will guide everything we do:

     Use the least harsh method that is likely to work.

This means:

     •  If you can prevent a problem by managing the environment,
        do that before correcting the behaviour.

     •  If you can teach with rewards, do that before using
        punishment.

     •  If you need to interrupt a behaviour, do it calmly—not
        with anger or force.

We don't need to scare dogs to train them. We don't need to hurt
them. We just need to be clear, consistent, and patient.

                              ───

                  POSITIVE REINFORCEMENT IN ONE SENTENCE

When your puppy does something you like, something good happens.

That's it. That's the foundation.

Over time, your puppy thinks: "Listening to my human makes good
things happen. I should try that again."

This is teaching from the inside out. Your dog wants to behave
well—not because they fear the consequence of behaving badly, but
because good behaviour works for them.

                              ───

                  WHY PUNISHMENT OFTEN BACKFIRES

Punishment may stop a behaviour in the moment. But it rarely
teaches what to do instead.

Here's what happens in your dog's brain when you punish:

     1. Something bad happens
     2. They feel fear or confusion
     3. They try to avoid the bad thing
     4. But they don't understand WHY it was bad

The result? Your dog learns to avoid getting caught—not to
avoid the behaviour itself. They chew shoes when you're not
watching. They jump on guests when you're in another room.
They've learned that YOU are the variable, not the behaviour.

Even worse, punishment can:

     •  Create fear and anxiety
     •  Damage trust between you and your dog
     •  Teach your puppy to hide behaviours rather than stop them
     •  Make learning slower and more confusing
     •  Associate YOU with bad feelings
     •  Cause aggression in some dogs

A puppy that is scared is not learning.
A puppy that feels safe is always learning.

THE TIMING PROBLEM:

For a dog to connect a consequence with a behaviour, the
consequence must happen within 1-2 seconds. Most "punishment"
happens too late. You come home, find a chewed shoe, and
scold your dog. But they chewed it hours ago. They have no
idea why you're angry. They just know you're scary sometimes.

That guilty look? It's not guilt. It's fear. Your dog is
responding to YOUR energy—your tense body, your angry voice—
not to a memory of chewing the shoe.

Consider this example: You can force a dog into a "down" position
by stepping on their leash until they lie down. Or you can lure
them down with a treat until they choose to lie down.

Both get the same result. But one builds trust and teaches the
dog to think. The other teaches them to comply out of discomfort.

Which dog do you want?

WHAT TO DO INSTEAD:

     •  Manage the environment so mistakes can't happen
     •  Interrupt unwanted behaviour calmly, then redirect
     •  Reward the behaviour you want to see
     •  Be patient—learning takes time

                              ───

                  MANAGING THE ENVIRONMENT: YOUR SECRET WEAPON

Here's a truth that will save you enormous frustration:

     You cannot train what you cannot control.

If your puppy has free access to shoes, they will chew shoes.
If your puppy can rush out the front door, they will rush out
the front door. If your puppy can reach the trash, they will
get into the trash.

This isn't a training failure. It's a management failure.

Your secret weapon is controlling the environment:

     •  Use baby gates to limit access to rooms
     •  Keep shoes in closets
     •  Use a leash indoors when you can't supervise
     •  Crate your puppy when you can't watch them
     •  Put tempting items out of reach

Prevention is easier than correction. Every time your puppy
practises an unwanted behaviour, that behaviour gets stronger.
Every time you prevent the behaviour, you buy time to teach a
better one.

                              ───

                  YOUR PUPPY IS NOT TRYING TO BE DIFFICULT

Let me be very clear about this:

     Your puppy is not stubborn.
     Your puppy is not manipulative.
     Your puppy is not trying to "test" you or "be dominant."

Your puppy is doing the best they can with the skills they
currently have.

If a behaviour is happening, it means one of three things:

     1. The puppy doesn't understand what you want yet
     2. The environment is too difficult or distracting
     3. The behaviour is being reinforced somehow

All three of these can be changed. That's good news.

When your puppy does something wrong, don't ask "Why is my dog
being bad?" Instead, ask:

     "What does my puppy need right now?"
     "How can I set them up to succeed?"
     "What am I accidentally rewarding?"

                              ───

                  THE GOAL OF TRAINING

The goal of training is not perfection.

The goal is:

     •  Communication—you and your dog understand each other
     •  Trust—your dog feels safe with you
     •  Safety—your dog can navigate the world without danger
     •  Shared understanding—you have a relationship, not just rules

A well-trained dog is not a robot who obeys every command without
thought. A well-trained dog is a companion who understands how to
live peacefully in a human world.

That's what we're building together.


                              ───

                  COMMON MISTAKES NEW OWNERS MAKE

Before we move on, let's address the most common mistakes I see:

MISTAKE #1: Expecting too much, too soon

Your puppy is a baby. They arrived on this planet recently.
They don't know the rules of your house, your neighbourhood,
or human society. Give them time. Weeks. Months. Years.

MISTAKE #2: Inconsistency

"Sometimes I let them on the couch, sometimes I don't."
"Sometimes I reward sits, sometimes I ignore them."

Inconsistency confuses dogs. They can't learn patterns if
the patterns keep changing. Decide on your rules and stick
to them—everyone in the household, every time.

MISTAKE #3: Too much freedom too fast

A puppy with full run of the house will make mistakes. They
will chew, have accidents, and develop bad habits. Freedom is
earned gradually, as they prove they can handle it.

MISTAKE #4: Treating symptoms, not causes

Your puppy is barking? They're probably bored or under-exercised.
Your puppy is destructive? They probably have too much freedom.
Your puppy won't listen? The environment is probably too hard.

Always ask: what need is not being met?

MISTAKE #5: Anthropomorphising

Dogs are not humans in fur coats. They don't feel spite, revenge,
or guilt the way we do. They don't "know what they did." When we
interpret their behaviour through human emotions, we usually get
it wrong—and train them wrong as a result.

Love your dog as a dog. Meet their needs as a dog. That's true
respect.

                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 2

               The First 24–72 Hours: Setting the Tone
                    ─────────────────────────────

Bringing a puppy home is like the first day of school mixed with
a sleepover. Everything is new—for both of you. These first hours
set the tone for your puppy's entire life in your home.

                              ───

                  FIRST STOP: THE POTTY SPOT

Before your puppy explores the living room, before they meet the
family, before anything else—take them to the outdoor spot where
you want them to go potty.

This might seem trivial, but it matters. You're establishing a
pattern from the very first moment.

Picture this: You've just arrived home. Your puppy has been in
the car for who knows how long. Their bladder is probably full.
Their nose is overwhelmed with new smells. And you're about to
carry them through your beautiful home with its new carpet...

See where this is going?

The first thing you do—literally, before going inside—is take
them to the potty spot. Every. Single. Time.

Stand still. Let them sniff. Be boring. Wait.

If they go, praise them gently. "Good puppy. Good potty." Keep
it calm. If they don't, no stress—you'll try again soon. The
point is to start building the association: outside = bathroom.

This one habit, started on day one, will save you weeks of
cleanup later.

                              ───

                  KEEP THINGS CALM AND PREDICTABLE

It's tempting to invite everyone over to meet the new puppy right
away. Resist that urge.

I know—everyone wants to see the puppy. Your mum is texting.
Your friends are asking. The neighbours are curious. But here's
what's happening in your puppy's world:

Yesterday, they were with their mother and littermates. They knew
the smells, the sounds, the routine. Today, everything they knew
is gone. Every single thing is new. New humans. New sounds. New
surfaces under their feet. New food. New sleeping arrangements.

This is a lot for a baby brain to process.

For the first day or two, keep things low-key:

     •  Let your puppy get used to just you and your immediate
        household
     •  Avoid overwhelming them with too many new people
     •  Keep voices calm and movements slow
     •  Let them explore at their own pace
     •  Give them a quiet place to retreat when overwhelmed

Your puppy's brain is processing an enormous amount of new
information. The smells, sounds, surfaces, and faces are all
completely foreign. Give them time to adjust.

WHAT IF MY PUPPY SEEMS SCARED OR SHUT DOWN?

Some puppies bounce into a new home like they own the place.
Others freeze, hide, or refuse to eat. Both are normal.

If your puppy seems nervous:

     •  Don't force interaction—let them come to you
     •  Sit on the floor at their level
     •  Speak in soft, calm tones
     •  Offer treats but don't push if they won't take them
     •  Give them a covered crate or quiet corner to retreat to

This is not a broken puppy. This is a puppy who needs time.
Most will blossom within a few days once they feel safe.

Calm and predictable is the name of the game.

                              ───

                  NO TOUCH, NO TALK, NO EYE CONTACT

This is one of the most powerful techniques for meeting dogs—
whether your own puppy or someone else's.

When a new person enters your home or approaches your dog:

     •  NO TOUCH: Don't reach out to pet them immediately
     •  NO TALK: Don't make excited sounds or say their name
     •  NO EYE CONTACT: Don't stare directly at them

WHY THIS WORKS:

Humans greet with touch, eye contact, and excitement. Dogs
don't. In dog language, direct eye contact from a stranger
can feel threatening. Reaching toward them invades their space
before they've had a chance to gather information about you.

Dogs greet by SMELL first. They approach, sniff, and gather
data. Only after they've decided you're safe do they allow
touch.

When you ignore a dog at first, you let them approach on their
terms. You let them sniff your legs and shoes. You let them
decide you're okay.

THE RIGHT WAY TO GREET A DOG:

     1. Ignore the dog completely at first
     2. Keep your body relaxed, not leaning toward them
     3. Let them sniff you without acknowledging them
     4. When they're calm and have finished investigating,
        you can calmly acknowledge them
     5. If you pet them, start with the chest or side—not
        the top of the head

TEACH YOUR GUESTS THIS TOO:

When people visit, ask them to ignore your puppy at first.
No squealing, no immediate petting. This prevents over-excited
greetings and teaches your puppy that visitors aren't a big deal.

                              ───

                  INTRODUCING THE CRATE OR SAFE AREA

The crate should feel like a cozy bedroom—not a punishment zone.

On day one:

     •  Leave the crate door open
     •  Toss treats inside and let your puppy wander in and out
     •  Don't close the door yet
     •  Praise calm behaviour inside the crate
     •  Put a soft bed or blanket inside

The goal is for your puppy to choose the crate on their own. If
you force them in and slam the door, you're creating a negative
association that will haunt you for weeks.

Short, positive sessions. Patience. Let them learn that the crate
is safe.

                              ───

                  FIRST GENTLE SOCIAL EXPERIENCES

Your puppy's first experiences shape how they view the world.
Every new thing they encounter is being filed in their brain as
either "safe" or "scary."

Keep initial encounters positive but brief:

     •  Meeting another pet? Keep it short. Let them sniff.
        Separate before anyone gets overwhelmed.

     •  Hearing a vacuum for the first time? Turn it on briefly
        from another room while giving treats.

     •  A loud truck goes by? Stay calm. Your reaction teaches
        them how to react.

Your energy matters. If you're tense and worried, your puppy
notices. If you're calm and matter-of-fact, they learn that new
things are just part of life.

                              ───

                  INTRODUCING YOUR PUPPY TO OTHER FAMILY DOGS

If you already have a dog at home, the first meeting matters.

BEFORE THEY MEET:

     •  Exercise both dogs first—tired dogs are calmer dogs
     •  Plan the introduction on neutral ground if possible
     •  Have two handlers, one for each dog
     •  Keep both dogs on loose leashes

THE FIRST MEETING:

     •  Start at a distance where neither dog is reacting
     •  Let them see each other without immediate interaction
     •  Gradually move closer if both are calm
     •  Let them sniff briefly, then separate
     •  Reward calm behaviour from both dogs

AT HOME:

     •  Supervise ALL interactions at first
     •  Separate them when you can't watch
     •  Give each dog their own space (crate, bed, bowls)
     •  Some growling or correction from the older dog is normal
     •  Break up play if it gets too rough

If either dog seems stressed, anxious, or aggressive, slow down.
Some relationships take weeks to develop. There is no rush.

                              ───

                  INTRODUCING YOUR PUPPY TO THE FAMILY CAT

Contrary to popular belief, dogs and cats can live together
peacefully. It just takes a careful introduction.

THE GOLDEN RULES:

     •  Your cat's safety comes first
     •  Never force interaction
     •  Always provide the cat an escape route
     •  Be patient—this can take days, weeks, or even months

STEP-BY-STEP INTRODUCTION:

     1. Keep them completely separate at first. Let them smell
        each other through doors or by swapping bedding.

     2. Feed them on opposite sides of a closed door. This
        creates positive associations with each other's presence.

     3. First visual meeting: Keep your puppy on a leash. Let
        the cat observe from a safe distance (behind a gate or
        on a high surface). Reward your puppy for calm behaviour.

     4. If your puppy fixates, lunges, or barks at the cat,
        you're too close. Create more distance.

     5. Gradually decrease distance over multiple sessions.
        Days or weeks—not minutes.

     6. Never leave them unsupervised until you're 100% certain
        they're safe together. Even then, give the cat a
        dog-free zone they can always access.

YOUR CAT'S NEEDS:

     •  Keep food, water, and litter in puppy-free zones
     •  Provide high perches or baby-gated rooms
     •  Cat doors that only the cat can use work well
     •  Never let your puppy chase the cat—not even "playfully"

With patience and management, many dogs and cats become friends.
But some will only ever tolerate each other, and that's okay too.

                              ───

                  POOL AND WATER SAFETY

If you have a pool, pond, or live near water, safety is critical.

THE DANGER:

     •  Puppies can fall in and not know how to get out
     •  Even dogs who can swim can drown from exhaustion
     •  Cold water, currents, and steep edges add risk

PREVENTION:

     •  Keep pool areas fenced whenever possible
     •  Supervise 100% when your puppy is near water
     •  Never assume they "know how to swim"
     •  Consider a pool alarm or pet-specific motion detector

TEACHING POOL SAFETY:

     1. WAIT until your puppy is at least 4 months old and
        comfortable with new experiences

     2. INTRODUCE water gradually—shallow, warm, calm water first

     3. SHOW them the exit. Walk them to the pool steps or ramp
        repeatedly so they learn where to get out

     4. PRACTISE supervised exit drills from different spots

     5. NEVER throw a puppy into water. Let them choose to enter.

Some breeds (like Bulldogs) are not built for swimming and should
never be left near water unsupervised—even as adults.

When in doubt, use a dog life jacket.

                              ───

                  UNDERSTANDING FEAR PERIODS

This is something many new puppy owners don't know about, and it
can save you a lot of confusion.

Puppies go through "fear periods"—windows of time when they are
extra sensitive to scary experiences. A bad experience during
these times can create lasting fears.

THE FIRST FEAR PERIOD: Around 8–12 weeks old

This is when puppies naturally leave their mother and start
exploring the world. They are wired to be cautious—it's a
survival instinct.

During this time:

     •  Your puppy may seem nervous about things that seem
        perfectly harmless to you
     •  A loud noise, a strange person, or an unfamiliar object
        can make a big impression
     •  Positive experiences are extra impactful—but so are
        negative ones

THE SECOND FEAR PERIOD: Around 6–14 months old

This happens during adolescence. Your previously confident puppy
may suddenly seem nervous about things they were fine with before.

     •  Don't panic—this is normal
     •  Don't force them to confront things they're scared of
     •  Create positive associations with patience

WHAT TO DO DURING FEAR PERIODS:

     •  Do NOT hide your puppy away. Continue socialisation.
     •  Make every new experience extra positive
     •  If they seem scared, create distance. Don't force
        interaction.
     •  Keep sessions short and always end on a positive note
     •  Use treats and play to create good associations

One bad experience doesn't ruin a dog forever. But many negative
experiences during fear periods can create anxiety that's harder
to undo.

                              ───

                  ADJUSTMENT TAKES TIME

Every dog takes a few days to show their true personality.

Some puppies are extra hyper when they first arrive—everything
is exciting and new. Others are extra quiet and reserved—they're
processing and feeling uncertain.

Don't make too many judgments in the first few days. Your puppy
is calibrating to their new home.

By day three or four, you'll start to see who they really are.
That's when the real training begins.

                              ───


                    ─────────────────────────────
                            PART TWO

                      ESSENTIAL MANAGEMENT
                    ─────────────────────────────

The chapters in this section cover the practical systems that
prevent problems before they start. But first, a foundational
concept that applies to everything that follows:

                              ───

                  RULES, BOUNDARIES, AND LIMITATIONS

Before we dive into specific management strategies, let's talk
about the foundation of a well-balanced dog:

     Rules, Boundaries, and Limitations.

This sounds strict, but it's actually an act of love. Dogs
THRIVE with structure. Without it, they feel anxious and
uncertain. With it, they feel secure.

RULES are things your dog must always do or never do:

     •  No jumping on people
     •  Sit before eating
     •  Wait at doorways

BOUNDARIES are limits on where your dog can go:

     •  Stay off the couch (or only with permission)
     •  This room is off-limits
     •  Stay behind this line when the door opens

LIMITATIONS are restrictions on how long or how much:

     •  Playtime ends when I say so
     •  You get this much food, not more
     •  We walk for this long, then we're done

WHY THIS MATTERS:

In the wild, dogs live with constant structure. The pack has
clear rules. Everyone knows their role. There's no ambiguity.

When we bring dogs into our homes and give them no rules, we
create confusion. They don't know what's expected. They test
limits because no limits exist.

Clear rules = calm dog.

HOW TO SET RULES:

     1. Decide on your rules BEFORE problems start
     2. Make sure everyone in the house follows the same rules
     3. Be consistent—every single time
     4. Enforce calmly, not angrily
     5. Reward compliance generously

You're not being mean. You're being clear. And clarity is
kindness.

                              ───


                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 3

            Building a Daily Routine That Trains for You
                    ─────────────────────────────

One of the biggest mistakes new puppy owners make is thinking
that training only happens during "training sessions."

In reality, your puppy is learning all day long. Every walk,
every meal, every nap, every interaction teaches them something.

A good daily routine does most of the training work for you.

                              ───

                  WHY ROUTINE MATTERS SO MUCH

Puppies feel safest when life is predictable. When they roughly
know what comes next, they:

     •  Relax more easily
     •  Learn faster
     •  Make fewer bad choices
     •  Sleep through the night more readily
     •  Handle being alone better

You don't need a military schedule. You just need a rhythm.

                              ───

                  THE ORDER THAT WORKS: EXERCISE, DISCIPLINE, AFFECTION

One of the most powerful concepts in dog training is getting
the ORDER of activities right.

Many people give their dogs affection first—cuddles, treats,
excitement the moment they walk in the door. Then they try to
train. Then maybe some exercise if there's time.

This order often creates problems.

THE BETTER ORDER:

     1. EXERCISE FIRST
        Drain physical and mental energy before asking for focus.
        A tired dog is a trainable dog.

     2. DISCIPLINE SECOND
        "Discipline" doesn't mean punishment. It means structure:
        training, rules, boundaries, expectations. This is the
        work of building good habits.

     3. AFFECTION LAST
        Affection is the reward. It comes after the exercise
        and structure are in place. This way, you're rewarding
        the calm, balanced state—not the chaos.

WHY THIS WORKS:

When a dog is physically satisfied, they can focus mentally.
When they've worked for something, the reward means more.
When affection comes after calm behaviour, you reinforce calm.

Most humans do it backward—showering affection on excited,
unfocused dogs. This rewards the excitement.

Try flipping the order. You'll be amazed at the difference.

                              ───

                  THE BASIC PUPPY RHYTHM

Most healthy puppies naturally fall into this cycle:

     Wake up → Go potty → Move and explore → Train or play
     → Eat → Calm down → Sleep

This repeats many times throughout the day.

If your puppy is misbehaving—biting more, zooming around, barking,
ignoring you—one of these needs probably wasn't met.

Before correcting behaviour, always ask:

     "What does my puppy need right now?"

Maybe they're overtired. Maybe they need to go out. Maybe they
haven't used their brain yet today.

                              ───

                  MORNING: STARTING THE DAY RIGHT

Always start with a potty break. Puppies wake up with full
bladders.

After potty, give them time to:

     •  Sniff around
     •  Walk or stretch
     •  Play lightly

This burns off the first burst of energy and makes the rest of
the morning easier.

Feeding time is a training opportunity. Ask for one or two simple
behaviours before putting the bowl down—a sit or eye contact. This
builds calm habits around food.

                              ───

                  PRIMARY TRAINING SESSIONS

Primary training sessions are planned, focused moments where you
teach specific skills.

Examples:

     •  Sit, down, stay
     •  Come when called
     •  Look at me
     •  Tricks

For puppies, keep these short: 5 to 10 minutes is usually enough.

It's better to stop early while things are going well than to
push until your puppy is tired or frustrated. End on a win.

                              ───

                  SECONDARY TRAINING SESSIONS

Secondary training sessions happen when life happens. They're
unplanned moments that require you to snap into training mode.

Examples:

     •  Your puppy barks at a noise
     •  They get too excited when someone walks by
     •  They grab something they shouldn't

Instead of getting upset, step in calmly:

     •  Call them to you
     •  Ask for a simple behaviour they know
     •  Reward calmness

These moments are incredibly valuable. They teach your puppy how
to handle real-life situations—not just perfect training setups.

                              ───

                  NAPS ARE NOT OPTIONAL

Puppies need a lot of sleep. Much more than most people expect.

An overtired puppy often looks like a "naughty" puppy:

     •  Biting more
     •  Zooming around uncontrollably
     •  Ignoring cues
     •  Barking at nothing

If things feel out of control, try a nap before trying more
training.

A well-rested puppy learns better, listens better, and feels
better.

                              ───

                  EVENING AND WIND-DOWN TIME

As the day slows down, your routine should slow down too.

     •  Avoid intense play right before bed
     •  Focus on calm interaction
     •  Take a final potty break
     •  Follow a predictable bedtime routine

This helps your puppy understand that nighttime is for resting.

                              ───

                  DOGS LEARN BEST AFTER EXERCISE

A dog that has burned off energy is more primed to absorb new
concepts.

Before teaching something new, give your puppy some physical
activity first—a walk, a play session, or some running around.

A tired puppy is a focused puppy.


                              ───

                  SAMPLE PUPPY SCHEDULES

These are examples—adjust based on your puppy's age and needs.

8-12 WEEK OLD PUPPY:

     6:30 AM   Wake up, potty break
     6:45 AM   Breakfast, light play
     7:30 AM   Nap (crate or pen)
     9:30 AM   Wake, potty, short training (5 min)
     10:00 AM  Play, explore
     10:30 AM  Nap
     12:00 PM  Wake, potty, lunch
     12:30 PM  Supervised free time or socialisation outing
     1:30 PM   Nap
     3:30 PM   Wake, potty, play
     4:00 PM   Nap
     5:30 PM   Wake, potty, dinner
     6:00 PM   Family time, calm interaction
     7:00 PM   Wind-down activity (chew, puzzle)
     7:30 PM   Nap
     9:00 PM   Final potty break
     9:30 PM   Bedtime in crate

Note: Young puppies may need potty breaks at night too.

4-6 MONTH OLD PUPPY:

     6:30 AM   Wake up, potty break
     7:00 AM   Morning walk or play (15-20 min)
     7:30 AM   Breakfast
     8:00 AM   Training session (10 min)
     8:30 AM   Rest or independent time
     12:00 PM  Lunch, potty break
     12:30 PM  Midday walk or activity
     1:00 PM   Rest
     3:30 PM   Short training or puzzle toy
     5:00 PM   Evening walk (20-30 min)
     6:00 PM   Dinner
     6:30 PM   Family time, calm interaction
     8:00 PM   Light play or grooming practice
     9:30 PM   Final potty break
     10:00 PM  Bedtime

As your puppy matures, naps decrease and awake periods lengthen.
But even adult dogs benefit from routine and predictability.


                              ───

                  WHEN ROUTINE ISN'T POSSIBLE

Life isn't always predictable. Travel, illness, visitors, and
emergencies happen. When routine breaks down:

     •  Focus on the essentials: potty, food, sleep
     •  Use management (crate, gates) more heavily
     •  Lower your training expectations temporarily
     •  Return to routine as soon as possible

Dogs are resilient. An occasional disruption won't ruin your
training. What matters is the overall pattern, not perfection.


                              ───


                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 4

             Potty Training Without Losing Your Mind
                    ─────────────────────────────

Potty training is usually the first big challenge people face
with a new puppy. It can feel frustrating and personal.

It isn't personal. Your puppy isn't being stubborn or disrespectful.
They're simply learning how the world works.

                              ───

                  THE MOST IMPORTANT RULE

Here it is, very simply:

     If your puppy has an accident, it means they had
     too much freedom too soon.

Potty training is not about teaching a trick. It is about
management.

Your puppy learns where to go through:

     •  Repetition (same spot, every time)
     •  Timing (going out at the right moments)
     •  Being in the right place at the right time

                              ───

                  WHEN PUPPIES NEED TO GO OUT

Puppies need a potty break:

     •  Immediately after waking up
     •  After eating
     •  After drinking
     •  After playing
     •  After training
     •  Every 30–60 minutes when awake (sometimes more)

If you're wondering, "Do they need to go out right now?"

The answer is probably yes.

It is always better to go out too often than not often enough.

                              ───

                  PICKING A POTTY SPOT

Choose one outdoor spot and use it consistently.

     •  Take your puppy to the same area every time
     •  Stand still
     •  Give them time to sniff
     •  Be boring and patient

When they go:

     •  Praise calmly
     •  Give a treat if you like
     •  Then move on

The reward comes after they finish—not while they're trying.

                              ───

                  SUPERVISION IS EVERYTHING

When your puppy is awake and indoors, they should be:

     •  Actively supervised by you, OR
     •  In a crate, pen, or small puppy-proofed area

Loose puppies have accidents. Supervised puppies learn.

Signs your puppy may need to go:

     •  Sniffing the floor
     •  Circling
     •  Suddenly wandering away
     •  Becoming restless

If you see these signs, calmly take them outside immediately.

                              ───

                  WHAT TO DO IF YOU CATCH AN ACCIDENT

If you catch your puppy in the act:

     •  Interrupt gently ("Oops" or "Ah-ah")
     •  Pick them up or lead them outside
     •  Let them finish outside
     •  Reward when they do

Do not shout. Do not scare them.

Scaring a puppy only teaches them to hide when they go—which
makes your job much harder.

                              ───

                  WHAT TO DO IF YOU FIND AN ACCIDENT LATER

If the accident already happened:

     •  Clean it thoroughly with an enzyme cleaner
     •  Say nothing to the puppy
     •  Move on

Your puppy cannot connect punishment after the fact with the
accident. They don't understand why you're upset.

Focus on prevention, not punishment.

                              ───

                  REGRESSION IS NORMAL

Even when things seem to be going well, accidents may happen
again. This can be due to:

     •  Growth spurts
     •  New environments
     •  Too much freedom
     •  Changes in routine

Progress is not a straight line.

Your goal is fewer accidents over time—not perfection overnight.
Most puppies are not truly reliable until 8–12 months of age.

                              ───

                  BELL TRAINING FOR POTTY BREAKS

You can teach your puppy to ring a bell to signal they need to
go outside. This gives them a way to communicate with you.

How to teach it:

     1. Hang a bell near the door at your puppy's nose height
     2. Every time you go out, guide their nose or paw to ring it
     3. Start by rewarding any contact with the bell
     4. Gradually shape it into a deliberate ring
     5. Always take them out immediately after they ring it

Eventually, your puppy will ring the bell from anywhere in the
house to tell you they need to go.

                              ───

                  NIGHTTIME POTTY TRAINING

At night:

     •  Limit water before bed (an hour or two before)
     •  Take your puppy out right before sleep
     •  Keep nighttime trips calm and boring—no play
     •  Return them straight to bed

Nighttime is for sleeping, not for fun.

                              ───

                  CELEBRATE THE WINS

Every successful potty trip is a win.

Focus on what your puppy is doing right. Potty training improves
faster when you stay patient and consistent.


                              ───

                  POTTY TRAINING TROUBLESHOOTING

Even with perfect management, problems arise. Here are solutions
to the most common issues:

PROBLEM: "My puppy goes outside, then comes in and has an accident."

This is frustrating, but common. What's happening:

     •  They got distracted by smells, sounds, or exploring
     •  They didn't fully empty their bladder
     •  Coming inside relaxes them—and triggers the urge

Solution: Stay outside longer. Be boring. Wait for them to go
TWICE before coming in. The moment you get inside, watch them
like a hawk for the next 10-15 minutes.

PROBLEM: "My puppy only goes in one specific spot in the house."

Good news: this shows they understand the concept of a "potty
spot"—they've just chosen the wrong one.

Solution: Block access to that area completely. Clean it with
an enzyme cleaner. Take them outside more frequently. When they
go outside, celebrate enthusiastically.

PROBLEM: "My puppy was doing great, then suddenly regressed."

Regression is normal and can happen because of:

     •  Growth spurts (body growing faster than bladder control)
     •  New environment or routine change
     •  Stress (new people, travel, vet visits)
     •  Too much freedom given too soon

Solution: Go back to basics. More supervision, more trips
outside, less freedom. They'll bounce back.

PROBLEM: "My puppy won't go potty outside in bad weather."

Some puppies hate rain, cold, or wind. They've associated
outside with discomfort.

Solution: Go out anyway—briefly. Reward ANY attempt to go.
Build positive associations with weather: treats, play, calm
praise. Use a covered area if possible. With time, they adapt.

PROBLEM: "I live in a flat/apartment and can't get outside quickly."

This makes potty training harder but not impossible.

Options:

     •  Use a puppy pad as a temporary transition
     •  Create a consistent "potty spot" on a balcony
     •  Carry your puppy to the outdoor spot to save time
     •  Consider a grass patch or indoor potty system

The principles remain the same: consistency, timing, reward.


                              ───


                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 5

               Crate Training and Learning to Relax
                    ─────────────────────────────

A crate is not a cage. When used correctly, it is your puppy's
bedroom, safe space, and off switch.

                              ───

                  WHY CRATE TRAINING MATTERS

Crate training helps with:

     •  Potty training (dogs don't like to soil where they sleep)
     •  Preventing destructive behaviour
     •  Teaching your puppy how to relax
     •  Building comfort with being alone
     •  Keeping your puppy safe when unsupervised

                              ───

                  THE CRATE SHOULD NEVER FEEL LIKE PUNISHMENT

Dogs naturally seek small, enclosed spaces to rest. A crate taps
into that instinct—but only if introduced properly.

A crate should feel:

     •  Safe
     •  Predictable
     •  Comfortable

Never put your puppy in the crate when you're angry. Never use
the crate as punishment. The crate must always be a positive place.

                              ───

                  INTRODUCING THE CRATE THE RIGHT WAY

Start slow.

     •  Leave the crate door open
     •  Toss treats inside
     •  Let your puppy walk in and out freely
     •  Praise calm behaviour inside

At first, do not close the door. The goal is for your puppy to
choose the crate on their own.

                              ───

                  MEALS AND CHEWS IN THE CRATE

One of the easiest ways to build positive feelings about the
crate is to use food.

     •  Feed meals inside the crate
     •  Give safe chew toys during crate time
     •  Use stuffed food toys (like frozen Kong toys)

When your puppy is busy chewing, they are relaxed. Relaxation is
what we want them to associate with the crate.

                              ───

                  CLOSING THE DOOR AND BUILDING DURATION

Once your puppy is comfortable going inside:

     •  Close the door for just a few seconds
     •  Stay nearby
     •  Open the door before they show stress

Gradually increase:

     •  Time inside
     •  Distance from you
     •  Normal household noises

Always aim to release your puppy while they are calm—not while
they're crying or scratching.

                              ───

                  USING THE CRATE FOR NAPS

Many puppies struggle to settle on their own. The crate helps
them switch off.

Signs your puppy needs a nap:

     •  Increased biting
     •  Zooming around
     •  Ignoring cues
     •  Barking for no clear reason

A well-timed crate nap can prevent many behaviour problems before
they start.

                              ───

                  WHEN A PUPPY CRIES IN THE CRATE

Some crying is normal, especially early on.

Before responding, ask:

     •  Have they gone potty recently?
     •  Are they tired?
     •  Are they safe?

If you're confident their needs are met, give them a moment to
settle. Many puppies will settle within a few minutes.

If crying escalates into panic, you may have moved too fast. Go
back a step and make things easier next time.

                              ───

                  CRATE VS. PEN VS. PUPPY-PROOFED ROOM

Different setups work for different homes.

     •  Crates are great for sleep and short alone periods
     •  Pens allow more movement but still limit freedom
     •  Puppy-proofed rooms work well once your puppy is
        more reliable

The goal is always the same: prevent mistakes while your puppy
is learning.

                              ───

                  THE CRATE IS A TOOL, NOT A LIFESTYLE

As your puppy grows and learns to relax on their own, crate time
usually decreases.

A well-trained dog can choose to rest calmly without confinement.
The crate helps you get there.


                              ───

                  CRATE TRAINING TROUBLESHOOTING

PROBLEM: "My puppy cries the moment the door closes."

You've moved too fast. The puppy isn't ready for a closed door
yet.

Solution: Go back to basics. Feed all meals in the crate with
the door open. Play crate games: toss treats in, let them come
out. Build value for being inside before adding the closed door.

PROBLEM: "My puppy was fine, then suddenly hates the crate."

This can happen after a bad experience (illness, being crated
too long, a scary noise while crated) or during fear periods.

Solution: Don't force it. Make the crate more comfortable. Feed
high-value treats inside. Cover it with a blanket for security.
Rebuild the positive association slowly.

PROBLEM: "My puppy chews the crate or tries to escape."

This is panic, not normal adjustment. Your puppy is experiencing
genuine distress.

Solution: Stop crating until you've addressed the underlying
issue. This might be separation anxiety (see next chapter) or
a crate that's too small/uncomfortable. Consult a trainer.

PROBLEM: "My puppy won't go in voluntarily."

They haven't learned that the crate is a good place yet.

Solution: Make the crate the best place in the house. All meals
inside. Best chews inside. Treats scattered inside randomly. Let
them discover that the crate is where good things happen.

PROBLEM: "How long can I crate my puppy?"

This depends on age:

     •  8-10 weeks: Max 1 hour during the day
     •  10-12 weeks: 1-2 hours
     •  3-4 months: 3-4 hours
     •  5-6 months: 4-5 hours
     •  6+ months: 6 hours maximum (less is better)

Overnight is different—puppies can sleep longer. But during
the day, crates should be for naps, not long-term storage.


                              ───


                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 6

            Separation Anxiety and Learning to Be Alone
                    ─────────────────────────────

One of the most important skills a dog can learn is how to be
okay when alone.

This doesn't mean loving it. It doesn't mean being perfectly
happy every second. It means being calm, safe, and able to cope.

                              ───

                  ALONE TIME IS A SKILL, NOT A PERSONALITY TRAIT

Some people think dogs are either "independent" or "clingy." In
reality, being alone is a learned skill.

Your puppy is not trying to control you or manipulate you. They
are simply unsure about what happens when you leave.

Our job is to show them, step by step, that being alone is safe
and temporary.

                              ───

                  START SMALL AND BUILD SLOWLY

The biggest mistake people make is leaving for too long, too soon.

Start with tiny absences:

     •  Step into another room for a few seconds
     •  Close the door briefly
     •  Come back calmly

Repeat this many times a day.

Gradually increase:

     •  Time away
     •  Distance
     •  Real-life absences (taking out the trash, checking mail)

                              ───

                  CALM DEPARTURES AND CALM RETURNS

How you leave and return matters more than you think.

When leaving:

     •  Do not make a big deal
     •  No long goodbyes
     •  No apologetic voices

When returning:

     •  Wait for calm behaviour before greeting
     •  Greet gently
     •  Resume normal life

Big emotional goodbyes and greetings make alone time feel more
intense than it needs to be. Leaving and returning should feel
boring and normal.

                              ───

                  USING FOOD AND CHEWS TO SUPPORT ALONE TIME

Give your puppy something positive when you leave:

     •  Safe chew toys
     •  Food-dispensing toys
     •  A meal given just before you leave

Chewing releases calming hormones. It gives your puppy something
to focus on while you're gone.

                              ───

                  WHAT'S NORMAL AND WHAT'S NOT

Normal during learning:

     •  Brief whining
     •  Mild restlessness
     •  Settling after a short time

Concerning signs:

     •  Intense, nonstop barking
     •  Trying to escape the crate or room
     •  Drooling, pacing, or panic
     •  Not eating food when alone

If you see signs of panic, slow down. Reduce the duration of
alone time and rebuild more gradually.

                              ───

                  USING CAMERAS OR MONITORS

Cameras can be helpful tools. They allow you to:

     •  Check how long your puppy takes to settle
     •  See whether whining escalates or fades
     •  Return home at the right moment

Use cameras to gather information—not to stress yourself out.

                              ───

                  EXERCISE AND MENTAL STIMULATION BEFORE LEAVING

A puppy who hasn't moved, played, or used their brain will
struggle more with alone time.

Before leaving, make sure your puppy has had:

     •  A potty break
     •  Some physical movement
     •  Mental stimulation (training or puzzle toys)

A tired puppy is more likely to rest.

                              ───

                  OPTIMIZE CRATE TIME FOR SUCCESS

A key strategy: have your puppy take naps in the crate during
the day when they're naturally tired.

This builds up their experience of being content and alone in
the crate. The more time they spend in the crate in a relaxed
state, the more accepting they become.

                              ───

                  INDEPENDENCE IS A GIFT

Teaching your puppy to be alone is not unkind. It is one of the
greatest gifts you can give them.

A dog who can relax alone:

     •  Feels safer in the world
     •  Handles changes and travel better
     •  Is easier to live with long-term

Progress may be slow, but it is worth it.


                              ───

                  SEPARATION ANXIETY VS. NORMAL DISTRESS

It's important to distinguish between:

NORMAL DISTRESS: Your puppy whines or barks when you leave but
settles within 10-15 minutes. They may not love being alone, but
they cope. This improves with gradual training.

SEPARATION ANXIETY: Intense panic that doesn't improve. Signs
include: continuous barking for hours, destructive attempts to
escape, urinating/defecating even in housetrained dogs, refusal
to eat, drooling, pacing, self-harm.

True separation anxiety is a clinical condition. It's not your
puppy being "naughty." It's genuine panic that requires:

     •  A behaviour modification plan (usually very gradual)
     •  Possibly medication (consult a veterinary behaviourist)
     •  Professional guidance

If you suspect true separation anxiety, don't wait. Early
intervention is much more effective than letting it become
ingrained.

IMPORTANT: If your dog only has issues when you leave—not when
crated with you home—it's separation-related, not crate-related.
Solving the wrong problem wastes time.


                              ───

                  THE REMOTE WORK TRAP

If you work from home—or spend most of your time at home—your
puppy may never learn to be comfortable alone. This is one of
the most common causes of separation anxiety.

Deliberately practise absences:

     •  Leave the house for short periods, even when you don't
        need to
     •  Crate your puppy during work hours sometimes
     •  Create physical separation within your home

A puppy who only learns to exist in your presence will struggle
badly when circumstances change—when you return to an office,
travel, or simply run errands.

Prevention is far easier than rehabilitation. Build alone time
into your routine from the start, even if you're home all day.

                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 7

                Rewards, Games, and "Dog Currency"
                    ─────────────────────────────

Dogs don't work for free. Just like people, they are motivated
by things they like.

In training, we call these "rewards"—and you can think of them
as your dog's currency. If you understand what your dog values,
training becomes much easier.

                              ───

                  WHAT COUNTS AS A REWARD?

A reward is anything your puppy wants more of:

     •  Food (treats, kibble, tiny snacks)
     •  Toys (balls, tug toys, frisbees)
     •  Play (chasing, tug, gentle wrestling)
     •  Attention (praise, petting, happy voices)
     •  Access to the environment (going forward on a walk,
        sniffing a bush, greeting someone)

Different dogs value different things. The same dog may value
different rewards at different times of day.

Your job is to notice: "What does my puppy care about right now?"

                              ───

                  FOOD: THE EASIEST TRAINING CURRENCY

Food is usually the easiest reward to start with, especially for
puppies.

Why food works so well:

     •  It's fast to deliver
     •  It's clear and unmistakable
     •  It's easy to repeat many times
     •  Puppies need to eat anyway

Treats should be very small—pea-sized or even smaller. The reward
is not the amount of food. The reward is the timing.

If your puppy does the right thing and you reward them immediately,
they learn faster.

                              ───

                  THE POWER OF THE WORD "YES"

Use "Yes" as a marker—a word that tells your puppy: "That's
exactly what I wanted!"

     •  Say "Yes" the moment they do the right thing
     •  Follow immediately with a treat

Over time, your puppy learns that "Yes" means good things are
coming. It becomes a bridge between the behaviour and the reward.

                              ───

                  MIXING REWARDS KEEPS TRAINING INTERESTING

You don't have to use the same reward every time. Variety helps.

     •  Sometimes food
     •  Sometimes a toy
     •  Sometimes praise and movement
     •  Sometimes freedom to go sniff

For example:

     •  Puppy comes when called → treat
     •  Puppy sits calmly while a bike passes → praise
     •  Puppy ignores another dog → freedom to explore

All of these tell your puppy: "That choice worked."

                              ───

                  GAMES ARE TRAINING TOO

Play is not separate from training. It IS training.

Games like fetch, tug, or chase can teach:

     •  Impulse control
     •  Listening under excitement
     •  Coming back to you
     •  Dropping objects on cue

Rules make games useful:

     •  The game starts when you say so
     •  The game pauses if the puppy bites too hard
     •  The game resumes when the puppy calms down

This teaches your puppy that fun comes from cooperation.

                              ───

                  USING THE ENVIRONMENT AS A REWARD

One of the most powerful rewards is access to the world.

     •  Puppy walks nicely → you keep moving
     •  Puppy sits before the door → the door opens
     •  Puppy looks at you instead of pulling → they get to sniff

This is real-life training. Your puppy learns that good behaviour
makes life better—not just that treats appear.

                              ───

                  WHEN REWARDS "STOP WORKING"

Sometimes your puppy won't take food or toys. This usually means:

     •  They are too excited
     •  They are too nervous
     •  The environment is too challenging

This is not stubbornness.

In these moments:

     •  Increase distance from the distraction
     •  Lower your expectations
     •  Ask for easier behaviours
     •  Let your puppy observe instead of perform

Training is not about winning. It's about setting your puppy up
to succeed.

                              ───

                  INTERMITTENT REINFORCEMENT: THE WEANING SECRET

Many people worry: "Will my dog only listen if I have treats?"

The answer is no—if you wean off treats correctly.

THE PRINCIPLE:

Once your puppy understands a behaviour, you don't need to reward
every single time. In fact, random rewards actually make the
behaviour stronger.

This is called "intermittent reinforcement."

Think of it like a slot machine: you don't win every time, but
the occasional win keeps you playing. Dogs work the same way.

HOW TO WEAN OFF TREATS:

     1. FIRST: Reward every correct response (build the behaviour)

     2. THEN: Reward most responses, but skip a few
        (Example: reward, reward, skip, reward, skip, skip, reward)

     3. GRADUALLY: Make rewards less predictable
        Keep your dog guessing—they'll try harder!

     4. REPLACE: Start using praise, play, and life rewards
        more often
        ("Good dog!" + ear scratch instead of a treat)

IMPORTANT NOTES:

     •  Don't wean off too early—most people rush this
     •  Keep treats handy for new environments or distractions
     •  Continue rewarding heavily when raising difficulty
     •  Random rewards keep behaviour strong for life

The goal is a dog who listens because they trust you, enjoy
working with you, and know that good things sometimes happen—
not because they're scared or bribed.

This takes time. Expect to use treats regularly for the first
6 months to a year. That's normal.

                              ───

                  PHASING REWARDS OVER TIME

At the beginning, reward often. Very often.

Later, once your puppy clearly understands a behaviour:

     •  Reward less frequently
     •  Mix up when rewards appear
     •  Occasionally surprise them with a bigger reward

This keeps behaviours strong without needing food forever.

But remember: removing rewards too early is one of the most
common mistakes. Build the habit first. Then slowly fade.


                              ───

                  WHY SOME DOGS SEEM "UNMOTIVATED"

Some owners say, "My dog doesn't care about treats" or "My dog
isn't food motivated." This is almost never true.

What's actually happening:

STRESS: A stressed dog can't eat. If your puppy won't take food
outside but devours treats at home, stress is the issue. Work at
a distance where they can think.

HEALTH: A dog that's full, sick, or uncomfortable won't eat.
Train before meals, not after. Rule out medical issues.

VALUE: Kibble doesn't compete with squirrels. Use better treats
in harder situations. Real meat, cheese, or liver beats dry
biscuits.

SATIATION: If you've been training for 20 minutes and they're
slowing down, they're full. End the session.

Every dog is motivated by something. Your job is to find what
and when.


                              ───

                  REAL-WORLD REWARD EXAMPLES

Let me give you concrete scenarios:

TEACHING "SIT":

     •  Initially: Treat every single time they sit
     •  Later: Treat 4 out of 5 sits
     •  Eventually: Treat occasionally, praise always
     •  Real life: "Sit before dinner" → dinner is the reward

TEACHING "COME":

     •  Initially: Treat + party every time they come
     •  Later: Sometimes treat, sometimes just play
     •  Eventually: Sometimes they come and get to go back to
        what they were doing (powerful!)
     •  Real life: Coming inside means breakfast

LEASH WALKING:

     •  Initially: Treat every few steps of nice walking
     •  Later: Treat less often, but keep them guessing
     •  Eventually: Moving forward is the reward
     •  Real life: "Loose leash means we keep moving"

The pattern: Start generous. Fade gradually. Use life rewards.


                              ───


                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 8

          Focus Skills: Name, "Look at Me," and "Leave It"
                    ─────────────────────────────

Before your puppy can learn fancy tricks or walk politely in busy
places, they need one core skill: attention.

If your puppy can't focus on you, nothing else really works.

This chapter covers three foundation skills that make everything
else possible.

                              ───

                  TEACHING YOUR PUPPY THEIR NAME

Your puppy's name should mean one simple thing:

     "Good things happen when I pay attention."

How to teach it:

     1. Say your puppy's name once
     2. The moment they look at you, say "Yes" and give a treat
     3. Repeat many times throughout the day
     4. Start in easy places (like your living room)

Important rules:

     •  Don't repeat the name over and over
     •  Don't say the name when your puppy is in trouble
     •  Don't use the name as a warning

If your puppy hears their name and ignores it, the situation was
too hard. Make it easier next time.

                              ───

                  "LOOK AT ME": YOUR EMERGENCY BRAKE

"Look at me" teaches your puppy to move their eyes away from
distractions and back to you.

How to teach it:

     1. Hold a treat near your eyes
     2. When your puppy looks at your face, say "Yes" and reward
     3. Gradually move the treat away, so they look at you
        without seeing food first

Practise this everywhere:

     •  At home
     •  In the yard
     •  On quiet walks
     •  Near mild distractions

This skill is incredibly useful when:

     •  Another dog appears
     •  Your puppy fixates on something
     •  You need to regain control quickly

                              ───

                  "LEAVE IT": LEARNING TO MAKE BETTER CHOICES

"Leave it" teaches your puppy that ignoring something leads to
something better.

How to teach it:

     1. Hold a treat in a closed fist
     2. Let your puppy sniff and investigate
     3. The moment they pull away or look at you, say "Yes"
     4. Reward with a different treat from your other hand

Important detail: The reward never comes from the thing they are
leaving alone. This teaches them that leaving things leads to
better outcomes.

Over time, practise with:

     •  Treats on the floor
     •  Toys
     •  Sticks and leaves
     •  Food smells outside
     •  Garbage on walks

"Leave it" is not about punishment. It's about teaching impulse
control.

                              ───

                  THE LEAVE IT + LOOK AT ME COMBO

This is where the magic happens. Combine both skills.

     1. Present a distraction (treat on the floor)
     2. Say "Leave it"
     3. When they look up at you, say "Yes" and reward

This teaches your puppy: "The way I get what I want is to pay
attention to you—not to chase the distraction."

Practise with treats first, then toys, then real-world items.

                              ───

                  PROOFING IN REAL LIFE

Just because your dog leaves a treat alone doesn't mean they'll
leave garbage alone on a walk.

Dogs don't automatically generalize. Every new context is a new
lesson.

Practise "leave it" in the real world:

     •  With sticks and leaves
     •  With food dropped on the floor
     •  With other dogs passing by
     •  With squirrels in the distance

                              ───

                  DISTANCE MATTERS MORE THAN DIFFICULTY

If your puppy cannot respond to a cue, you are probably too close
to the distraction.

     •  Back up
     •  Create distance
     •  Lower expectations

Training is not about forcing attention. It's about making
attention possible.

                              ───


                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 9

             Sit, Down, Stay, and Basic Obedience
                    ─────────────────────────────

These are often called "basic obedience," but they are much more
than basic. Sit, down, and stay are tools that help your puppy
pause, think, and make safer choices in everyday life.

                              ───

                  TEACHING SIT THE KIND WAY

Sit is usually the first cue puppies learn.

How to teach it:

     1. Hold a treat near your puppy's nose
     2. Slowly lift the treat upward and slightly back
     3. As their head goes up, their bottom naturally goes down
     4. The moment they sit, say "Yes" and reward

Do NOT push your puppy's body into position. Let them figure it
out. Learning through choice builds confidence.

                              ───

                  TEACHING DOWN WITHOUT FORCE

Down is similar to sit, but many puppies find it harder at first.

How to teach it:

     1. Start from a sit
     2. Lower the treat straight down to the floor
     3. Slowly pull it forward along the ground
     4. When your puppy follows and lies down, say "Yes" and reward

Some puppies need extra time. Be patient. This is a vulnerable
position, and comfort matters.

                              ───

                  LURE TRAINING: THE ART OF GUIDING WITH TREATS

Lure training means coaxing your dog into a position by having
them follow a treat with their nose.

     •  Keep the treat close to their nose
     •  Let them nibble as they follow
     •  Move slowly—rushing creates confusion
     •  Say "Yes" the moment they hit the position

Once they understand the motion, gradually phase out the lure
and introduce a hand signal.

                              ───

                  HAND SIGNALS: YOUR SECRET WEAPON

Dogs are masters at reading body language. In fact, many dogs
respond to hand signals faster than verbal cues.

WHY HAND SIGNALS MATTER:

     •  Dogs naturally read visual cues better than words
     •  Useful in noisy environments
     •  Helpful as dogs age and may lose hearing
     •  Creates clearer communication

HOW TO INTRODUCE HAND SIGNALS:

     1. Start with lure training (treat guides their body)
     2. Once reliable, make the same motion WITHOUT a treat
        in your hand
     3. Reward with a treat from your other hand
     4. Your hand motion becomes the signal

COMMON HAND SIGNALS:

     •  SIT: Palm facing up, lift hand upward
     •  DOWN: Palm facing down, lower hand to floor
     •  STAY: Open palm facing the dog, like a "stop" sign
     •  COME: Arms open wide, or hand sweeping toward your body

You can create any hand signals you like. Just be consistent.

Once your dog knows both the word AND the hand signal, they can
respond to either one—or both together for extra clarity.

                              ───

                  THE SIT-DOWN-UP-STAND FLOW

Here's a powerful exercise that teaches your puppy to follow
your guidance and builds their focus. Dogs can learn multiple
things at once when you chain them together.

THE FLOW:

     1. SIT: Lure your puppy's nose upward. As their head goes
        up, their bottom goes down. "Yes!" and reward.

     2. DOWN: From sit, lower the treat straight to the floor
        and slowly pull it forward. When they lie down, "Yes!"
        and reward.

     3. UP: From down, raise the treat upward. Your puppy rises
        back into a sit. Call this "Up!" and reward.

     4. STAND: From sit, move the treat forward and slightly up.
        Your puppy stands on all four feet. "Yes, stand!" and
        reward quickly before they walk forward.

WHY THIS WORKS:

     •  Teaches your puppy to follow your lead
     •  Builds mental focus and engagement
     •  Keeps training sessions dynamic and fun
     •  Plants seeds for more advanced skills later

Mix up the order once they understand each piece. Keep them
guessing—dogs love a little variety!

                              ───

                  ADDING WORDS AFTER THE MOVEMENT

In the beginning, focus on the movement—not the word.

Once your puppy reliably follows the lure:

     •  Say "Sit" just before you move your hand
     •  Say "Down" just before you lower the treat

This helps your puppy connect the word with the action.

                              ───

                  STAY: TEACHING PATIENCE IN SMALL STEPS

Stay is not about freezing forever. It's about waiting calmly.

Start small:

     1. Ask for a sit
     2. Pause for one second
     3. Say "Yes" and reward
     4. Release with a word like "Okay"

Three types of stay to practise:

     1. Duration: staying for longer periods of time
     2. Distance: staying while you walk away
     3. Distraction: staying when something exciting happens

Build each one separately before combining them.

Always release your puppy clearly. Stay should never feel endless.

                              ───

                  STAY AT THE CRATE DOOR

A great way to practise: ask your puppy to stay when you open
the crate door.

     •  Open the door slightly
     •  If they stay, reward
     •  If they move, close the door and try again
     •  Open more, reward, repeat

This teaches: the door opening doesn't mean "rush out."

                              ───

                  END ON A WIN

Always try to finish training sessions with something your puppy
can do well. This keeps training positive and builds enthusiasm
for the next session.


                              ───

                  PROOFING: THE MISSING PIECE

Many people teach a behaviour at home and wonder why it falls
apart outside. The reason: they never "proofed" it.

PROOFING means gradually teaching a behaviour in:

     •  Different locations
     •  Different levels of distraction
     •  Different situations

EXAMPLE: PROOFING "SIT"

     Level 1: Sit in your living room (no distractions)
     Level 2: Sit in the garden
     Level 3: Sit on the pavement outside your house
     Level 4: Sit at the park with no dogs around
     Level 5: Sit at the park with dogs in the distance
     Level 6: Sit at the park with dogs nearby
     Level 7: Sit at the dog park

Each level is a new learning experience. Don't skip steps. If
your dog fails at level 5, go back to level 4.

THE 80% RULE:

If your puppy can do the behaviour correctly 8 out of 10 times,
you can make it slightly harder. If they're failing more than
half the time, you've moved too fast.


                              ───

                  COMMON OBEDIENCE MISTAKES

MISTAKE #1: Saying the cue before they know the behaviour

Don't say "Sit!" while luring. Teach the behaviour first (silent
luring), then add the word. Otherwise, you're just making noise.

MISTAKE #2: Repeating cues

"Sit. Sit. SIT. SIT!" This teaches your dog that "Sit" doesn't
really mean anything until you say it four times loudly.

Solution: Say it once. If they don't respond, help them (lure
or reposition). Then try again.

MISTAKE #3: Releasing without a cue

Your dog learns that stay ends whenever they feel like it.

Solution: Always release with a word like "Okay!" or "Free!"

MISTAKE #4: Making sessions too long

Puppies have short attention spans. 3-5 minutes is plenty.
Multiple short sessions beat one long, frustrating session.

MISTAKE #5: Only training during "training time"

The best training happens during daily life. Ask for a sit
before dinner. Ask for a down before opening the door. Ask for
a stay before crossing the street.


                              ───


                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 10

            Come When Called: The Most Important Skill
                    ─────────────────────────────

Come when called—also known as "recall"—is one of the most
important skills your dog will ever learn.

A reliable recall can save your dog's life.

                              ───

                  WHY RECALL MATTERS SO MUCH

Consider these scenarios:

     •  Your dog runs toward a busy road. "Come!" could save
        their life.

     •  Your dog approaches an aggressive dog. "Come!" gets
        them out safely.

     •  You need to leave the park. "Come!" makes it possible.

This skill must be practised early, often, and in many different
places.

                              ───

                  START BY PAIRING THEIR NAME WITH SOMETHING GREAT

Say your puppy's name in an excited voice. When they look at you
or move toward you, say "Yes" and reward.

This is the beginning of recall—their name means "Come to me,
good things happen."

                              ───

                  THE BACK-AND-FORTH GAME

If you have two people, this is one of the best ways to teach
recall:

     •  Person 1: "Puppy, come!" → reward when they arrive
     •  Person 2: "Puppy, come!" → reward when they arrive
     •  Repeat, increasing distance gradually

Make it enthusiastic. Make it fun. Dogs should BOLT to you.

                              ───

                  MAKE COMING TO YOU THE BEST THING EVER

Always reward recall generously—especially in the beginning.

Critical rule: Never call your puppy and then do something they
don't like (bath, nail trim, leaving the park, going in the crate).

Coming to you should always feel like winning the lottery.

                              ───

                  USE AN EXCITED VOICE

Be animated. Be enthusiastic. Use a higher pitch.

If your puppy seems hesitant:

     •  Crouch down
     •  Open your arms
     •  Clap
     •  Run away from them

Dogs love to chase. Let them chase you.

                              ───

                  PRACTISE WHEN THEY'RE NOT EXPECTING IT

Recall matters most when your dog isn't expecting to be called.

Practise randomly throughout the day:

     •  Call them from another room
     •  Call them while they're sniffing
     •  Call them while they're mildly distracted

This teaches them that "Come!" is always relevant.

                              ───

                  THE WORKING DISTANCE PRINCIPLE

If your puppy doesn't come, they're not being stubborn. The
situation is too difficult.

     •  Increase distance from the distraction
     •  Make it easier
     •  As they succeed, gradually close the distance

Find the distance where they can still think—then work from there.

                              ───

                  AFTER THEY COME, LET THEM GO BACK

Don't always end the fun when they come to you.

Sometimes, after they come and get their reward, let them go back
to what they were doing.

This teaches: coming to you doesn't always mean fun is over.

                              ───

                  FOLLOW THROUGH

If you call and they don't come, don't just repeat the command
over and over.

     •  Go to them
     •  Gently guide them back to where you called from
     •  Reward

Don't let "Come!" become optional.


                              ───

                  RECALL MISTAKES TO AVOID

MISTAKE #1: Poisoning the cue

Every time you call "Come!" and then do something your dog
doesn't like—bath, crate, leaving the park—you weaken the cue.
Eventually, "Come!" means "something bad is about to happen."

Solution: If you need to do something unpleasant, go get your
dog instead of calling them.

MISTAKE #2: Repeating the cue

"Come! Come! Come! COME!!!"

This teaches your dog that "Come" is background noise. They
learn to wait until you really mean it.

Solution: Say it once. If they don't respond, go get them.

MISTAKE #3: Punishing a slow recall

Your dog finally comes to you after ignoring you for a minute.
You scold them for being slow. Now they've learned: coming to
you sometimes ends badly.

Solution: ALWAYS reward when they come—even if it took forever.
Work on speed separately, in easier environments.

MISTAKE #4: Only practicing in easy environments

Your living room is not the real world. If you only practise
recall at home, don't expect it to work at the dog park.

Solution: Practise in progressively harder environments:
garden → quiet street → busier areas → dog park (on long line).

MISTAKE #5: Chasing your dog

When your dog doesn't come and you chase them, you've started
a game. Dogs love being chased. Now they run away MORE.

Solution: Run AWAY from your dog. They'll chase you. Then
reward when they catch up.


                              ───

                  WHAT IF MY DOG WON'T COME AT THE DOG PARK?

The dog park is the hardest place for recall. Other dogs are
incredibly exciting. You are... less exciting.

The solution is not to practise recall AT the dog park—at least
not yet. Build recall in easier places first. When it's rock
solid, then test it with distractions.

At the dog park, use a long line (10-15 metres) until recall is
reliable. This keeps your dog safe while you train.

And remember: sometimes the environment is just too hard. That's
okay. Pick your battles. Train another day.


                              ───


                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 11

                    Doorway and Gate Safety
                    ─────────────────────────────

Doorways are one of the most important places to teach self-control.

An open door should never mean "run."

                              ───

                  WHY DOOR SAFETY MATTERS

Dogs can bolt out doors and run into traffic. This skill could
save your dog's life.

Door training is about impulse control and safety—not just
obedience.

                              ───

                  HOW TO PRACTISE DOOR SAFETY

     1. Ask for a sit near the door
     2. Begin to open the door slightly
     3. If your puppy moves, close the door
     4. When they stay still, open it a bit more
     5. Release them with "Okay" before they go through

The message: the door opening means nothing until YOU say so.

                              ───

                  ADD DISTRACTIONS

Once they're staying with the door open:

     •  Throw a treat or toy outside while they stay
     •  Practise "Look at me" with the door open
     •  Have someone walk by outside

                              ───

                  PRACTISE AT ALL DOORS

     •  Front doors
     •  Back doors
     •  Gates
     •  Car doors

Each new door is a new lesson. Dogs don't generalize automatically.

                              ───

                  ALWAYS USE A LEASH WHEN TRAINING NEAR DOORS

For safety, always have your puppy on leash when practicing door
stays—especially near roads.

Consider attaching the leash to your belt for extra security.

                    ─────────────────────────────
                            PART FOUR

                       COMMON CHALLENGES
                    ─────────────────────────────


                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 12

           Play Biting, Chewing, and Saving Your Stuff
                    ─────────────────────────────

If your puppy bites you, chews your shoes, or attacks your sleeves
like a tiny dinosaur—you are not alone.

This is normal puppy behaviour, not aggression, and not a sign
that you're doing something wrong.

                              ───

                  WHY PUPPIES BITE

Puppies use their mouths the way human babies use their hands.

They bite because:

     •  They are teething
     •  They are excited
     •  They are exploring
     •  They want to play
     •  They are overtired

Biting is communication. Our job is to guide it, not punish it.

                              ───

                  TEACHING BITE INHIBITION

Bite inhibition means learning how hard is too hard.

Puppies usually learn this from other dogs—when they bite too
hard, play stops.

You can teach the same lesson:

     •  If teeth touch skin, calmly stop interacting
     •  Freeze or gently step away
     •  Redirect to a toy
     •  Resume play when your puppy is calmer

The message: "Gentle play continues. Rough play ends."

                              ───

                  REDIRECT, DON'T WRESTLE

Hands are not chew toys.

Keep appropriate items nearby:

     •  Soft toys
     •  Tug toys
     •  Chew toys

If your puppy goes for your hands:

     •  Offer a toy instead
     •  Praise when they choose it
     •  Move your hands away calmly

Avoid wrestling with your puppy using your hands. This teaches
that biting people is part of the game.

                              ───

                  CHEWING IS A NEED, NOT A PROBLEM

Chewing helps puppies:

     •  Relieve teething pain
     •  Calm themselves
     •  Explore textures

Your job is not to stop chewing. It's to control what gets chewed.

     •  Put shoes away
     •  Use baby gates
     •  Limit access to tempting objects

Provide legal chewing options every day.

                              ───

                  USING CHEWS THE RIGHT WAY

Good chews:

     •  Are safe for your puppy's age
     •  Are given during calm moments
     •  Help your puppy settle

Chews are especially helpful:

     •  After walks
     •  During crate time
     •  When your puppy needs help relaxing

Always supervise new chews.

                              ───

                  OVERTIRED PUPPIES BITE MORE

Many biting problems are really sleep problems.

If biting suddenly increases, ask:

     •  When was the last nap?
     •  How long have they been awake?
     •  How stimulating was the environment?

A nap often solves what training cannot.

                              ───

                  KIDS AND PUPPY BITING

Children need clear rules around puppies:

     •  No rough play
     •  No running and screaming near the puppy
     •  Always have a toy between hands and teeth

Teach kids to freeze and call an adult if the puppy gets too
excited.

                              ───

                  DESTRUCTIVE CHEWING PREVENTION

Destructive chewing comes from too much freedom too soon.

     •  Keep your puppy on leash indoors when unsupervised
     •  Use a crate or pen when you can't watch them
     •  Puppy-proof your home thoroughly

Every time they chew something wrong, they're building a habit.
Prevent it before it starts.

                              ───

                  THIS PHASE DOES NOT LAST FOREVER

Biting and chewing improve gradually as your puppy:

     •  Gets adult teeth (around 6 months)
     •  Learns self-control
     •  Becomes better at settling

Stay consistent, calm, and patient.


                              ───

                  BREED CONSIDERATIONS

Some breeds are mouthier than others. This isn't bad behaviour
—it's genetics.

RETRIEVERS (Labs, Goldens) were bred to carry things in their
mouths. They naturally want to hold, carry, and mouth objects.
They may take longer to learn bite inhibition but often become
very gentle adults.

HERDING BREEDS (Border Collies, Shepherds, Corgis) were bred
to nip at heels to move livestock. They may naturally target
ankles, especially when excited or when children run.

TERRIERS were bred to catch and shake prey. They may grip
hard during tug and be persistent about biting during play.

GUARDIAN BREEDS may use their mouths less in play but need
careful handling around strangers and other dogs.

Know your breed. Adjust your expectations. But the training
principles remain the same: redirect, reward what you want,
and be patient.


                              ───

                  BITING TROUBLESHOOTING

PROBLEM: "My puppy bites HARD, not gentle mouthing."

Some puppies didn't learn bite inhibition from their littermates.
You'll need to teach it more deliberately.

Solution: Say "Ouch!" in a high-pitched voice and freeze. If
biting continues, leave the room for 30 seconds. Return calm.
Repeat consistently. They're learning: hard biting = fun ends.

PROBLEM: "My puppy targets my ankles and feet."

This is common in herding breeds and high-energy puppies. They're
treating you like something to be herded.

Solution: Stop moving. A still target is boring. Redirect to a
toy. Practise when they're calmer, not in motion.

PROBLEM: "Biting gets worse in the evening."

This is almost always overtiredness. Puppies need 18-20 hours
of sleep. If they've been awake for more than an hour, they may
be running on fumes—and expressing it with teeth.

Solution: Enforced nap in the crate. The biting will likely
stop after rest.

PROBLEM: "I've tried everything and nothing works."

If you're truly consistent and nothing is improving after
several weeks, consider:

     •  Is your puppy getting enough sleep?
     •  Is your puppy getting enough mental stimulation?
     •  Are all family members following the same rules?
     •  Consult a positive reinforcement trainer for guidance


                              ───


                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 13

                  Resource Guarding Prevention
                    ─────────────────────────────

Resource guarding is when a dog protects something they value—
usually with growling, snapping, or biting.

It's instinctive, but we can prevent it with early, positive
training.

                              ───

                  WHAT IS RESOURCE GUARDING?

Resource guarding means a dog feels the need to protect something:

     •  Food
     •  Toys
     •  Bones and chews
     •  Sleeping spots
     •  Sometimes even people

It's a natural survival instinct. But in a human home, it can
become dangerous.

Prevention is much easier than fixing it later.

                              ───

                  HAND FEEDING: THE FOUNDATION

Feed your puppy some meals directly from your hand.

This teaches them: "When people are near my food, good things
happen."

Your hands become associated with giving—not taking.

                              ───

                  TAKING AWAY AND GIVING BACK

Practise gently taking things away, then giving them right back.

     •  Take the food bowl → give a high-value treat → return
        the bowl

     •  Take the toy → give a treat → return the toy

Your puppy learns: "When humans take things, I often get something
even better—and I usually get my thing back anyway."

                              ───

                  ADDING VALUE

While your puppy is eating from their bowl, approach and drop
something delicious into it.

This teaches: "Humans approaching my food means bonus treats."

Do this regularly during puppyhood.

                              ───

                  NEVER PUNISH GUARDING SIGNALS

If your puppy growls or stiffens when you approach their food,
do NOT punish them.

Growling is communication. They're telling you they're uncomfortable.

Punishing the growl doesn't fix the feeling—it just removes the
warning. Next time, they might bite without warning.

Instead:

     •  Take a step back
     •  Make things easier
     •  Focus on positive associations
     •  Consult a professional if needed

                              ───

                  SIGNS TO WATCH FOR

Early warning signs of resource guarding:

     •  Freezing when you approach their food
     •  Eating faster when you come near
     •  Hard stares or stiff body
     •  Low growling
     •  Snapping or lunging

If you see these signs, slow down and focus on building positive
associations. Don't push through it.

                              ───

                  WHAT IF IT'S ALREADY A PROBLEM?

If your dog already guards resources aggressively, work with a
qualified, force-free trainer.

Do not attempt to "show them who's boss." This usually makes
guarding worse, not better.

Resource guarding is treatable, but it requires patience, expertise,
and a careful approach.


                              ───

                  THE MYTH OF "TAKING THINGS TO SHOW DOMINANCE"

Old-fashioned advice suggested taking things from your dog to
prove you're in charge. This approach often creates resource
guarding rather than preventing it.

Imagine if someone randomly took your dinner away to prove
they could. You'd start eating faster, hiding your food, or
getting defensive. Dogs feel the same way.

The goal isn't to prove you CAN take things away. The goal is
to make your dog feel confident that your approach means good
things—so they don't NEED to guard.


                              ───

                  PREVENTION CHECKLIST

To minimise resource guarding risk:

     ☐  Hand-feed meals regularly
     ☐  Add treats to the food bowl while eating
     ☐  Practise trading items (take toy → give treat → return toy)
     ☐  Don't take things away "just because"
     ☐  Give your puppy space during high-value chews
     ☐  Never punish growling
     ☐  If children are present, supervise ALL interactions with food
     ☐  Create positive associations, not confrontational ones

Prevention takes minutes. Rehabilitation takes months.


                              ───

                  SPECIAL NOTE: CHILDREN AND RESOURCE GUARDING

Children are at highest risk for resource guarding bites because:

     •  They approach food bowls unpredictably
     •  They grab toys without warning
     •  They don't read warning signals

If you have children:

     •  NEVER let them approach a dog eating or chewing
     •  Teach them to NEVER take things from the dog's mouth
     •  Supervise all interactions
     •  Feed the dog in a separate room if necessary
     •  Take ANY growling seriously—it's a warning

This isn't about having a "bad" dog. It's about understanding
that dogs are animals with natural instincts. Responsible
management keeps everyone safe.

                    ─────────────────────────────
                            PART FIVE

                     EXPLORING THE WORLD
                    ─────────────────────────────


                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 14

              Leash Walking and the First Real Walks
                    ─────────────────────────────

For many people, leash walking is where frustration starts.
Puppies pull, stop, bite the leash, zigzag, or suddenly refuse
to move.

This doesn't mean your puppy is "bad on leash." It means they're
new to the idea.

Walking on a leash is not natural. It is a skill that must be
taught.

                              ───

                  TRAINING WALKS VS. EXPLORATION WALKS

One of the most important ideas in leash training:

     Not every walk is a training walk.

There are two types:

     •  Training walks: short, focused, with rewards and guidance
     •  Exploration walks: slower, relaxed, focused on sniffing

Both are important.

If every walk is strict and demanding, your puppy gets frustrated.
If every walk is chaotic, they never learn. Balance is the goal.

                              ───

                  START INSIDE BEFORE GOING OUTSIDE

Before walking down the street, practise inside your home.

     •  Clip the leash on
     •  Walk a few steps
     •  Reward when your puppy follows
     •  Stop if they pull
     •  Resume when the leash is loose

Short indoor sessions remove distractions and help your puppy
understand the basic idea.

                              ───

                  THE GOAL IS A LOOSE LEASH, NOT A PERFECT HEEL

Don't aim for competition-style heelwork. Not yet.

The goal is simple:

     •  The leash stays loose
     •  Your puppy checks in with you occasionally
     •  Walking together feels calm and safe

If the leash is tight, pause. When it loosens, move again.

This teaches: pulling stops progress; calm movement works.

                              ───

                  WHEN PUPPIES FREEZE OR SIT DOWN

Many puppies suddenly stop walking, especially in new places.

This usually means:

     •  They are overwhelmed
     •  They are tired
     •  They are unsure

Do NOT drag them.

Instead:

     •  Wait a moment
     •  Encourage gently
     •  Use your voice or a treat
     •  Give them time to observe

Confidence grows from choice, not force.

                              ───

                  LEASH BITING AND ZOOMIES

Leash biting often happens when puppies are:

     •  Overstimulated
     •  Overtired
     •  Frustrated

If it happens:

     •  Stay calm
     •  Stop moving
     •  Hold the leash still
     •  Redirect to a sit or "look at me"
     •  Resume when calm

If it happens frequently, shorten walks and add more nap time.

                              ───

                  USING THE ENVIRONMENT AS A REWARD

Walking itself is a powerful reward.

     •  Puppy walks nicely → you keep moving
     •  Puppy pulls → you stop
     •  Puppy looks at you → praise and move forward

Sniffing is also a reward. Let them sniff when they're calm and
responsive.

                              ───

                  NEW PLACES CHANGE EVERYTHING

A puppy who walks well at home may struggle elsewhere.

New smells, sounds, and sights increase difficulty. That's normal.

Lower expectations in new environments:

     •  Shorter walks
     •  More breaks
     •  More rewards
     •  More patience

                              ───

                  SAFETY FIRST

Always use:

     •  A secure harness or collar
     •  A properly fitted leash
     •  Extra caution near roads and crowds

Your puppy's safety matters more than perfect walking.

                              ───

                  PROGRESS IS MEASURED IN CALM MOMENTS

Success is not how far you walk.

Success is how calm your puppy can be while walking.

Celebrate small improvements. They add up.


                              ───

                  ENERGY LEVELS AND PULLING: THE TRUTH

Here's something many trainers won't tell you directly:

If your dog is pulling constantly, the problem is probably not
technique. It's energy.

A dog with pent-up physical energy CANNOT walk calmly. Their
body is buzzing. Every smell is electric. Every movement catches
their eye. They're not ignoring your training—they're physically
incapable of calm because their needs haven't been met.

THE SOLUTION:

     1. Exercise BEFORE the training walk
     2. Play fetch, tug, or let them run in a garden
     3. Drain the excess energy first
     4. THEN practise calm leash walking

High-energy breeds—Border Collies, Huskies, Vizslas, Spaniels—
may need 30-60 minutes of exercise before they can focus on
leash manners. This isn't a flaw; it's how they're built.

Low-energy breeds may only need a short play session.

Know your dog. Meet their needs. Then training works.


                              ───

                  LEASH WALKING TROUBLESHOOTING

PROBLEM: "My dog pulls toward other dogs."

Your dog is excited and wants to greet. But constant on-leash
greetings teach them that pulling = meeting dogs.

Solution: Practise "look at me" when dogs appear. Reward for
choosing you over the other dog. Don't let them greet every
dog—being calm around dogs is the skill, not greeting.

PROBLEM: "My dog pulls toward smells."

Sniffing is not bad—it's mentally enriching. But YOU decide
when sniffing happens.

Solution: Walk until they're calm, then release them to sniff
as a reward. "Go sniff!" becomes a reward for walking nicely.

PROBLEM: "My dog lunges at bikes, joggers, or cars."

This is usually excitement, frustration, or fear—not aggression.

Solution: Create more distance. Practise "look at me" when
triggers appear far away. Reward calm behaviour. Gradually
decrease distance as they improve.

PROBLEM: "My dog won't walk—they just sit or lie down."

This is often fear or overwhelm, not stubbornness.

Solution: Don't drag them. Sit with them. Let them observe.
Use treats to encourage small steps. Go home if needed. Try
a less overwhelming environment next time.

PROBLEM: "Walks are exhausting and I dread them."

If every walk is a battle, you're both stressed. That's not
sustainable.

Solution: Shorten walks. Lower expectations. Focus on one
skill at a time. Remember: a short, calm walk is better than
a long, stressful one.


                              ───


                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 15

                Socialisation and Desensitisation
                    ─────────────────────────────

Socialisation is one of the most misunderstood parts of dog training.

Many people think it means letting their puppy meet as many dogs
and people as possible. In reality, good socialisation is about
experiences, not greetings.

The goal is not excitement. The goal is calm confidence.

                              ───

                  WHAT SOCIALISATION REALLY MEANS

A well-socialised dog is not one who wants to say hello to everyone.

A well-socialised dog can:

     •  See people, dogs, and movement
     •  Hear strange sounds
     •  Notice new environments
     •  Stay calm and curious instead of overwhelmed

Socialisation teaches your puppy: "This exists, and it's okay."

                              ───

                  QUALITY OVER QUANTITY

Ten calm, positive experiences are better than one overwhelming one.

Good socialisation looks like:

     •  Watching dogs play from a distance
     •  Seeing people walk by without greeting them
     •  Hearing traffic, sirens, or construction
     •  Experiencing wind, rain, and different surfaces

You do not need to rush.

                              ───

                  SCREENSAVER TRAINING: WATCHING THE WORLD

"Screensaver training" means letting your puppy observe the world
without asking for anything.

     •  Sit on a bench at a park
     •  Let them watch people, dogs, and bikes go by
     •  Don't constantly cue behaviours
     •  Let them look, sniff, and think
     •  Reward calm choices when they happen naturally

This teaches emotional regulation—one of the most valuable skills
a dog can have.

                              ───

                  WORKING DISTANCE: THE KEY TO SUCCESS

Every puppy has a distance where they can still think.

Too close to a distraction:

     •  Pulling
     •  Barking
     •  Freezing
     •  Ignoring cues

Far enough:

     •  Able to take treats
     •  Able to look at you
     •  Able to relax

Always start at a distance where your puppy can succeed. You can
get closer later.

                              ───

                  SOCIALISING WITH OTHER DOGS

Your puppy does NOT need to play with every dog they see.

In fact, greeting every dog can create frustration and
over-excitement.

Better goals:

     •  Walk past dogs calmly
     •  Sit and watch dogs from a distance
     •  Practise "look at me" around dogs

If they do play:

     •  Keep it supervised
     •  Choose well-matched dogs
     •  Keep sessions short and positive

                              ───

                  SOCIALISING WITH PEOPLE

People come in many forms:

     •  Hats and sunglasses
     •  Uniforms
     •  Children
     •  Wheelchairs and strollers
     •  People with beards or loud voices

Let your puppy observe first. If they choose to approach calmly,
reward that choice. Never force interaction.

                              ───

                  DESENSITISATION: SOUNDS, OBJECTS, AND STRANGE THINGS

Introduce your puppy to anything that might startle them later.

VACUUM CLEANERS:

     •  Show it before turning it on
     •  Let them investigate while it's off
     •  Turn on briefly at a distance
     •  Reward calm behaviour
     •  Gradually get closer

GARBAGE BAGS:

     •  Open one slowly while giving treats
     •  Pair the popping sound with rewards

BALLOONS:

     •  Let them see and sniff
     •  Reward curiosity, not fear

THUNDER:

     •  When you see lightning, start playing with your puppy
     •  Pair the boom with fun
     •  Create a positive association before fear develops

KITCHEN APPLIANCES:

     •  Can openers, blenders, coffee grinders
     •  Introduce sounds gradually with treats

The idea: pair strange things with good experiences BEFORE your
puppy becomes afraid.

                              ───

                  WEATHER IS PART OF SOCIALISATION

Wind, rain, storms—these are all experiences your puppy needs.

     •  Short outings in different weather
     •  Calm encouragement
     •  Plenty of breaks

Even uncomfortable weather days can be learning opportunities.

                              ───

                  WHEN SOCIALISATION GOES WRONG

If your puppy has a bad experience:

     •  Don't panic
     •  Create more distance next time
     •  Make future sessions easier

One mistake does not ruin a dog.

                              ───

                  UNDERSTANDING FEAR PERIODS

Fear periods are real developmental phases when your puppy's
brain is especially sensitive to negative experiences.

FIRST FEAR PERIOD (8-12 weeks):

This often happens right when you bring your puppy home. A bad
experience during this time can leave a lasting impression.
Your puppy may suddenly seem afraid of things they were fine
with before.

SECOND FEAR PERIOD (6-14 months):

This one catches people off guard. Your confident teenage dog
suddenly becomes scared of the vacuum, strangers, or dogs they
used to play with. This is normal brain development.

WHAT TO DO DURING FEAR PERIODS:

     •  Don't force scary interactions—create more distance
     •  Don't coddle or reward fearful behaviour either
     •  Stay calm and matter-of-fact: "That's just a truck"
     •  Keep exposing them to the world, but more gently
     •  Use high-value treats to create positive associations
     •  If something scares them, move away, let them calm down,
        then approach again at a greater distance

WHAT NOT TO DO:

     •  Don't flood them with scary things hoping they "get over it"
     •  Don't punish fearful behaviour
     •  Don't avoid the world entirely—this creates worse problems

Fear periods pass. But how you handle them matters.


                              ───

                  SOCIALISATION MISTAKES TO AVOID

MISTAKE #1: Thinking more is better

"We met 100 dogs this week!" But were they all positive? One
bad experience can outweigh ten good ones. Prioritise quality.

MISTAKE #2: Forcing interaction

Your puppy is hiding behind your legs, and someone says "Let
me pet them!" Forcing this creates fear, not confidence. Protect
your puppy's right to say no.

MISTAKE #3: Only positive environments

If every outing is exciting—dog park, pet shop, play date—your
puppy never learns calm. Include boring outings: sitting outside
a café, watching traffic, walking in a quiet neighbourhood.

MISTAKE #4: Stopping too soon

Socialisation isn't "done" at 16 weeks. It's an ongoing process.
Keep exposing your adolescent and adult dog to new experiences.

MISTAKE #5: Ignoring warning signs

Lip licking, yawning, turning away, low body posture—these are
signs of stress. If you see them, your puppy is over threshold.
Create more distance. End on a positive note.


                              ───

                  CALM IS THE GOAL

A calm puppy is a confident puppy.

Socialisation is not about creating excitement. It's about building
stability.

                              ───

                  CAR TRAVEL: A SOCIALISATION ESSENTIAL

Many puppies get car sick or anxious in vehicles. Early, positive
exposure helps.

START SMALL:

     •  Sit in the parked car with your puppy. Reward calm behaviour.
     •  Start the engine (but don't drive). Reward more.
     •  Take very short trips—just around the block.
     •  Gradually increase distance.

SAFETY:

     •  Never let your puppy ride loose in a moving car
     •  Use a crate, carrier, or dog-safe seatbelt/harness
     •  Keep windows mostly closed (or use window guards)
     •  Never leave a dog in a parked car in warm weather

CAR SICKNESS:

     •  Keep trips short at first
     •  Don't feed right before driving
     •  Let fresh air in
     •  Take breaks on longer trips
     •  Ask your vet if motion sickness continues

The goal: car rides should become neutral or positive, not stressful.

                              ───

                  EMERGENCY PREPAREDNESS

Plan ahead for emergencies. Natural disasters, evacuations, and
accidents happen.

ESSENTIALS:

     •  Microchip your puppy (and register the chip!)
     •  Keep ID tags on the collar at all times
     •  Have a recent photo of your dog on your phone
     •  Know which friends or family could take your dog in an
        emergency
     •  Find pet-friendly hotels along potential evacuation routes

EMERGENCY KIT:

     •  3-day supply of food and water
     •  Medications (if any)
     •  Leash and collar with ID
     •  Vaccination records
     •  First aid basics (bandages, antiseptic, tweezers)
     •  Comfort item (blanket or toy)

Don't wait until an emergency happens to prepare.


                              ───

                  INTRODUCING PUPPIES TO CATS

If you have cats, introducing your new puppy requires patience
and management. Done wrong, you can create a lifetime of stress.
Done right, dogs and cats can coexist peacefully—and sometimes
even become friends.

BEFORE THEY MEET:

     •  Create safe spaces where only the cat can go (high perches,
        baby gates, rooms the dog can't access)
     •  Let them smell each other's bedding before meeting
     •  Make sure your cat has escape routes in every room
     •  Exercise your puppy before introductions

THE FIRST INTRODUCTION:

     •  Keep your puppy on a leash
     •  Let the cat observe from a safe distance
     •  Reward your puppy for calm behaviour (looking away, sitting)
     •  Do NOT allow chasing under any circumstances
     •  Keep early sessions short—5 minutes maximum

BUILDING GOOD HABITS:

     •  Practise "leave it" with the cat as the distraction
     •  Reward your puppy for ignoring the cat
     •  Never force interactions—let the cat approach if they want
     •  Feed them in separate areas

RED FLAGS:

     •  Intense staring or fixation
     •  Stiff body, raised hackles
     •  Trembling (from excitement, not cold)
     •  Any chasing behaviour

If you see these, create more separation and work on impulse
control before trying again.

BREED CONSIDERATIONS:

Some breeds have higher prey drives and may struggle more:

     •  Sighthounds (Greyhounds, Whippets) were bred to chase
     •  Terriers were bred to catch small animals
     •  Some herding dogs may try to herd cats

This doesn't mean they can't live with cats, but you'll need to
be more careful and patient with training.

The goal isn't necessarily friendship—it's peaceful coexistence.
A dog who ignores the cat is a success.


                              ───

                  LIVING IN A MULTI-DOG HOUSEHOLD

If you have other dogs, introducing a new puppy requires
thoughtfulness.

BEFORE THE PUPPY ARRIVES:

     •  Consider your existing dog's temperament—do they like
        puppies? Some older dogs find puppies exhausting.
     •  Set up separate spaces so each dog can have alone time
     •  Have separate food bowls, beds, and toys

THE INTRODUCTION:

     •  Meet on neutral territory if possible (not your home)
     •  Keep both dogs on leash at first
     •  Allow sniffing but be ready to interrupt if tension rises
     •  Watch body language carefully

AT HOME:

     •  Supervise all interactions at first
     •  Separate them when you can't supervise
     •  Protect your older dog from being overwhelmed
     •  Puppies need to learn when older dogs say "enough"

COMMON ISSUES:

The older dog growls at the puppy: This is often appropriate
communication. The older dog is setting boundaries. As long as
it doesn't escalate to snapping or aggression, allow it.

The puppy won't leave the older dog alone: Enforce breaks.
Crate the puppy or separate them regularly so the older dog
gets rest.

Resource guarding between dogs: Feed separately. Remove
high-value items. Work on "drop it" and "leave it" with both.

Over time, most dogs adjust and form their own relationship.
Your job is to manage the early days and prevent bad habits
from forming.

                            PART SIX

                    FUN AND ADVANCED SKILLS
                    ─────────────────────────────


                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 16

                      Training Through Play
                    ─────────────────────────────

Training does not always have to look serious.

Some of the most powerful learning happens through play.

                              ───

                  FULFILLMENT: WHAT EVERY DOG TRULY NEEDS

Before we talk about games and tricks, let's talk about
fulfillment.

A fulfilled dog is a happy dog. An unfulfilled dog is often
a problem dog.

Dogs were bred for thousands of years to WORK. They herded
sheep, guarded homes, hunted prey, pulled sleds. Their bodies
and minds were built for challenge and purpose.

Today, most dogs live in apartments and houses with little
to do. They get food in a bowl. They sleep on soft beds. They
wait all day for their humans to come home.

This is comfortable. But it's not fulfilling.

SIGNS OF AN UNFULFILLED DOG:

     •  Destructive chewing
     •  Excessive barking
     •  Digging
     •  Hyperactivity
     •  Anxiety
     •  Obsessive behaviours

These aren't "bad dog" problems. They're often "bored dog"
problems.

THE FULFILLMENT FORMULA:

     EXERCISE + STRUCTURE + AFFECTION = BALANCED DOG

     •  EXERCISE drains physical energy and satisfies
        the body's need to move
     •  STRUCTURE (training, rules, jobs) satisfies the
        mind's need for purpose
     •  AFFECTION rewards the balanced state and strengthens
        the bond

If your dog is struggling with behaviour, ask yourself:

     "Is my dog truly fulfilled?"

Often, adding more structured activity—not more treats or
cuddles—is the answer.

                              ───

                  WHY PLAY MATTERS

Play helps puppies:

     •  Release energy in healthy ways
     •  Practise self-control while excited
     •  Learn to listen even when emotions are high
     •  Build trust and connection with you

A puppy who plays with rules learns faster than a puppy who is
only corrected.

                              ───

                  FETCH: MORE THAN JUST CHASING A BALL

Fetch is a great game, but only if it's structured.

How to teach fetch:

     1. Start in a quiet space
     2. Toss the toy a short distance
     3. Encourage your puppy to come back
     4. Reward when they return toward you

At first, focus on the return—not the drop.

You can trade:

     •  Toy for treat
     •  Toy for another throw

This teaches: "Coming back to my human makes the game continue."

                              ───

                  TUG: TEACHING CONTROL THROUGH EXCITEMENT

Tug is often misunderstood. It does not make dogs aggressive.

When done correctly, tug teaches control.

Rules for tug:

     •  The game starts on your cue
     •  The game stops if teeth touch skin
     •  The game pauses if your puppy ignores a cue

Teach "drop" by:

     •  Holding the toy still (making it boring)
     •  Waiting for release
     •  Rewarding when they let go

A calm restart is more important than winning.

                              ───

                  USING PLAY AS A REWARD

Play can replace food in many situations.

     •  Puppy comes when called → play begins
     •  Puppy sits calmly → throw the toy
     •  Puppy ignores a distraction → tug game starts

This is especially helpful for puppies who love movement more
than treats.

                              ───

                  KNOWING WHEN TO STOP

End play sessions:

     •  Before your puppy becomes overstimulated
     •  While things are still going well

A puppy who wants "just a little more" will be excited for the
next session.

                              ───

                  PLAY BUILDS THE RELATIONSHIP

When training feels fun, your puppy wants to participate.

Play teaches your puppy:

     •  You are interesting
     •  You are safe
     •  Listening leads to good things

That bond makes everything else easier.

                              ───


                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 17

                 Easy Tricks Anyone Can Teach
                    ─────────────────────────────

Tricks are not just cute party skills. They are powerful tools
for learning, confidence, and connection.

                              ───

                  WHY TRICKS ARE IMPORTANT

Trick training helps puppies:

     •  Use their brain in a positive way
     •  Learn how to follow cues
     •  Build body awareness
     •  Gain confidence
     •  Strengthen the bond with you

For kids, tricks teach patience, timing, and respect for the dog.

                              ───

                  RULES FOR KIDS AND PUPPIES

Before teaching any tricks, set clear rules:

     •  An adult should always supervise
     •  No yelling or rough handling
     •  One cue at a time
     •  Treats are given calmly, not thrown

                              ───

                  PAW / SHAKE

     1. Ask your puppy to sit
     2. Hold a treat in your closed fist
     3. Wait for them to touch your hand with their paw
     4. Say "Yes" and reward

Add the word "Paw" once they're doing it reliably.

                              ───

                  SPIN

     1. Hold a treat near your puppy's nose
     2. Slowly move it in a circle
     3. When they follow and turn around, reward

Practise both directions to keep them balanced.

                              ───

                  BOW

     1. Start with your puppy standing
     2. Lower a treat straight down between their front legs
     3. Reward when the front goes down and the back stays up
     4. Pull the treat away before they lie all the way down

                              ───

                  PAWS UP

     1. Lure your puppy to place their front paws on a low
        surface or your knee
     2. Reward while they hold the position
     3. Release clearly so they know when it's over

Great for photos and building confidence.

                              ───

                  ROLLOVER

     1. Start from a down
     2. Move the treat toward their shoulder, then around
     3. As they roll, say "Yes" and reward

Tip: Watch their hips. Roll toward the side their hips are shifted.

                              ───

                  PLAY DEAD

This is essentially "rollover" paused halfway. A real crowd-pleaser!

     1. Start from a down
     2. Lure slowly toward their shoulder (like rollover)
     3. But STOP when they're on their side
     4. Reward for staying still in that position
     5. Add the cue "Bang!" with a finger-gun gesture

The theatrical version: Wait a beat, then say "Okay!" to release.

                              ───

                  SPEAK

Some dogs do this naturally. Others need encouragement.

     1. Hold a treat your puppy REALLY wants
     2. Wait. Don't give it right away.
     3. Many puppies will eventually make a sound out of frustration
     4. The moment they bark or whine, say "Yes!" and reward
     5. Add the word "Speak" once they're doing it reliably

IMPORTANT: If your puppy is a problem barker, skip this trick.
You don't want to encourage barking in a dog who already barks
too much.

                              ───

                  CRAWL

A more advanced trick that's impressive and fun.

     1. Start from a down
     2. Hold a treat close to the floor, in front of their nose
     3. Slowly drag it forward
     4. If they stay low and scoot forward, reward!
     5. If they stand up, say "No" and try shorter distance

This one takes patience. Many dogs want to stand up to follow
the treat. Keep the treat low and reward small progress.

                              ───

                  HUG (WITH PERMISSION)

"Hug" is paws up on your shoulders.

Important rules:

     •  Only on cue
     •  Only with permission
     •  Never on strangers

Teach "paws up" first as a prerequisite.

                              ───

                  TRICKS DON'T CAUSE BAD MANNERS

Many people worry tricks like "paws up" will cause jumping.

They won't—if:

     •  Tricks only happen on cue
     •  Jumping without permission is calmly interrupted
     •  Clear rules are always followed

Dogs understand context when we are consistent.

                              ───

                  KEEP SESSIONS SHORT AND HAPPY

Trick sessions should be:

     •  5–10 minutes
     •  Ended on success
     •  Full of encouragement

If your puppy loses interest, stop. Learning happens best when
they want more.


                              ───

                  MORE TRICKS TO EXPLORE

Once you've mastered the basics, consider:

WEAVE: Walk forward while your dog weaves through your legs.
Start by luring them through one leg at a time, then chain them
together.

TOUCH: Dog targets your palm with their nose. Useful for
positioning, heeling, and redirecting attention.

PLACE: Go to a specific bed or mat and stay there. Great for
doorbell training and settling.

WAIT: Like stay, but less formal. Used for doorways, before
eating, or at street crossings.

BACK UP: Dog walks backward on cue. Start in a narrow hallway
where they can't turn around.

MIDDLE: Dog stands between your legs, facing forward. Great for
calming anxious dogs in busy places.

Each trick builds skills that transfer to real life.


                              ───

                  TRICKS BY BREED TENDENCY

Some tricks come easier to certain breeds:

RETRIEVERS often love: Fetch, carry, drop it, pick up items,
deliver objects to hand.

HERDING DOGS often excel at: Weave, spin, heel, complex chains
of behaviours, anything involving focus on you.

TERRIERS enjoy: Shake, paw, dig on cue, kill-the-toy games.

HOUNDS may prefer: Scent work, find it games, tracking.

GUARDIAN BREEDS may like: Place, wait, down-stay (they often
prefer calm, purposeful tasks to flashy tricks).

TOY BREEDS can do everything larger dogs can—don't underestimate
them. They often love spin, wave, and play dead.

Work WITH your dog's natural tendencies. If they love using their
nose, teach scent tricks. If they love using their paws, teach
paw-based tricks.


                              ───

                  THE MENTAL BENEFITS OF TRICK TRAINING

Trick training isn't just entertainment. It provides:

MENTAL STIMULATION: 15 minutes of trick training can tire a
dog as much as a 30-minute walk.

CONFIDENCE: Learning new things and succeeding builds self-
esteem in nervous dogs.

COMMUNICATION: Every trick is a conversation. You're building
a shared language.

PROBLEM-SOLVING: Dogs learn to think through challenges rather
than just react.

RELATIONSHIP: Working together on tricks strengthens your bond.

If your dog seems bored, hyperactive, or destructive, add more
mental work before adding more physical exercise. It often works
better.

                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 18

               When Things Go Wrong (And They Will)
                    ─────────────────────────────

Every puppy has bad days. Every owner has moments of frustration.

This does not mean you are failing. It does not mean your puppy
is "regressing forever."

It means you are training a real, living being.

                              ───

                  PROGRESS IS NOT A STRAIGHT LINE

Training rarely looks like steady improvement.

More often, it looks like:

     •  Two good days
     •  One confusing day
     •  A great breakthrough
     •  A sudden setback

This is normal.

Puppies grow, change, and go through phases. Skills that looked
solid yesterday may wobble today—especially in new environments
or during growth spurts.

                              ───

                  COMMON REASONS THINGS FALL APART

If your puppy suddenly seems "worse," check these first:

     •  Too little sleep
     •  Too much excitement
     •  Too many new experiences at once
     •  Too much freedom
     •  Expectations that are too high

Very often, the solution is not more discipline. It's making
things easier.

                              ───

                  TWO STEPS FORWARD, ONE STEP BACK

Think of training like climbing a hill.

Sometimes you move forward. Sometimes you slip back. What matters
is the overall direction.

When things feel hard:

     •  Reduce distractions
     •  Shorten training sessions
     •  Use better rewards
     •  Increase management

Going back to easier versions of an exercise is not failure. It
is good training.

                              ───

                  AVOID THE "PUSH THROUGH" TRAP

Pushing a tired, overwhelmed puppy to keep performing usually
makes things worse.

Signs you should stop:

     •  Puppy stops taking treats
     •  Puppy ignores cues repeatedly
     •  Puppy becomes mouthy or frantic
     •  Puppy shuts down or avoids you

Ending a session early protects your progress.

                              ───

                  YOUR PUPPY IS ALWAYS LEARNING SOMETHING

Even on bad days, learning is happening.

The question is: "What is my puppy learning right now?"

If frustration enters the picture, pause and reset. Calm guidance
teaches better lessons than anger ever will.

                              ───

                  BE KIND TO YOURSELF

You will make mistakes. Everyone does.

Training is not about perfection. It is about consistency over
time.

One rough day does not undo weeks of good work.


                              ───

                  REAL TALK: COMMON CRISES

Let me address some moments that make owners panic:

"MY PUPPY BIT SOMEONE"

Unless the bite drew blood and was unprovoked, this is probably
a normal puppy who got overexcited or scared. Don't panic.

     •  Review the context: Were they overwhelmed? Overtired?
     •  Increase management: leash, gates, supervision
     •  Work on impulse control: "leave it," "look at me"
     •  If bites are repeated or severe, consult a behaviourist

"MY PUPPY DESTROYED SOMETHING VALUABLE"

This hurts. But remember: it's a management failure, not a
character flaw. Your puppy doesn't know the difference between
a £10 shoe and a £200 one.

     •  Accept the loss (blame won't help)
     •  Puppy-proof better
     •  Restrict freedom until they've earned it

"MY PUPPY IS AGGRESSIVE"

True aggression in puppies is rare. What looks like aggression
is usually:

     •  Fear (the most common cause)
     •  Resource guarding (can be trained)
     •  Overexcitement (misread as aggression)
     •  Normal puppy biting (not aggression at all)

If you're genuinely concerned about aggression—not just normal
puppy behaviour—get a professional assessment. Don't wait.

"I'M REGRETTING GETTING THIS PUPPY"

This is more common than people admit. "Puppy blues" is real.
The exhaustion, the constant vigilance, the feeling that you've
made a huge mistake—these are normal.

     •  It gets better. Usually around 4-6 months, you'll start
        to see the light.
     •  Ask for help. A trusted friend, family member, or dog
        walker can give you a break.
     •  Connect with others. Puppy groups, online forums—you're
        not alone in this.

If feelings of regret persist beyond the first few months,
consider whether this is truly the right time for a dog in
your life. There's no shame in acknowledging a mismatch.


                              ───

                  WHEN TO ASK FOR HELP

If you feel stuck:

     •  Seek a qualified, reward-based trainer
     •  Ask for help early, not late
     •  Avoid advice that relies on fear or force

Good guidance should make things clearer, not scarier.

SIGNS YOU NEED PROFESSIONAL HELP:

     •  Biting that breaks skin (especially as the puppy ages)
     •  Growling or snapping when resources are approached
     •  Fear that prevents normal activities
     •  Aggression toward people or dogs
     •  Extreme anxiety when left alone
     •  You're feeling overwhelmed or unsafe

There's no prize for struggling alone. A good trainer can save
you months of frustration.


                              ───


                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 19

              From Puppy to Teenager (And Beyond)
                    ─────────────────────────────

At some point—usually when you start thinking, "Wow, this is
going really well"—your puppy will grow up a little more.

Welcome to adolescence.

                              ───

                  WHAT CHANGES AS PUPPIES GROW

As puppies mature, several things happen at once:

     •  Their bodies get stronger and faster
     •  Their confidence increases
     •  Their curiosity expands
     •  Their impulse control is still developing

This can look like:

     •  Selective hearing
     •  Testing boundaries
     •  More interest in the environment
     •  Temporary loss of focus

Your dog has not "forgotten everything." They are learning how
to exist in a bigger, more exciting world.

                              ───

                  WHY CONSISTENCY MATTERS MORE THAN EVER

During adolescence, dogs notice patterns very clearly.

If rules change:

     •  Jumping is sometimes allowed
     •  Pulling sometimes works
     •  Ignoring cues sometimes has no consequence

Those behaviours will grow.

This is the phase where gentle but consistent follow-through
matters most.

You don't need to be strict. You need to be predictable.

                              ───

                  KEEP TRAINING, JUST CHANGE THE STYLE

Training doesn't stop after puppyhood. It evolves.

Move toward:

     •  Intermittent rewards (not every time)
     •  Real-life reinforcement
     •  Skills practised during walks and daily life

Short refreshers keep behaviours strong.

                              ───

                  SOCIALISATION NEVER ENDS

Continue exposing your dog to:

     •  New places
     •  Different people
     •  Calm dogs
     •  Weather and seasonal changes

A well-adjusted adult dog is built through ongoing, low-pressure
experiences.

                              ───

                  MENTAL EXERCISE MATTERS AS MUCH AS PHYSICAL

As your dog grows, physical exercise alone is not enough.

Mental exercise includes:

     •  Training games
     •  Scent work
     •  Puzzle toys
     •  Learning new tricks
     •  Calm observation in busy places

A mentally satisfied dog is calmer, happier, and easier to live
with.

                              ───

                  FREEDOM IS EARNED GRADUALLY

As your dog proves they can make good choices, you can safely
give more freedom:

     •  More off-leash time in safe areas
     •  More time outside the crate
     •  More responsibility in the house

If mistakes return, reduce freedom temporarily. This is not
punishment—it's guidance.

                              ───

                  THE GOAL WAS NEVER PERFECTION

The goal of training was never to create a robot.

The goal was to build:

     •  Communication
     •  Trust
     •  Understanding
     •  A shared language

A good dog is not one who never makes mistakes. A good dog is
one who can recover, listen, and try again.

                              ───

                  LOOKING BACK

One day, you will look at your dog and realise:

     •  The biting stopped
     •  The chaos settled
     •  The routine feels natural
     •  You understand each other

That didn't happen because of one trick or one rule. It happened
because you showed up every day, guided your dog kindly, and
stayed consistent even when it was hard.


                              ───

                  EXERCISE, DISCIPLINE, AFFECTION

This phrase captures the balance every dog needs—and the order
matters.

EXERCISE comes first. A dog with pent-up physical and mental
energy cannot think clearly. They cannot focus. They cannot
settle. Meeting their exercise needs is not optional—it's the
foundation everything else rests on.

How much exercise? It depends:

     •  High-energy breeds may need 1-2 hours of activity daily
     •  Medium-energy breeds may need 30-60 minutes
     •  Lower-energy breeds may need 15-30 minutes
     •  Puppies: shorter, more frequent bursts with lots of rest

Mental exercise counts too. Sniffing, training, puzzle toys—
these tire a dog out just as much as running.

DISCIPLINE comes second. Not punishment—discipline. Structure.
Rules. Boundaries. Consistency.

Your dog needs to know:

     •  Where they're allowed and not allowed
     •  What behaviours are acceptable
     •  That the rules don't change

This isn't being harsh. It's being clear. Dogs relax when they
understand the structure. They become anxious when they don't.

AFFECTION comes last. Not because it's least important—but
because affection without exercise and discipline often creates
problems. The dog who gets endless cuddles but no structure may
become anxious, demanding, or confused about their role.

When all three are in balance, you get a calm, happy dog who
knows their place in your world.


                              ───

                  BREED EXPECTATIONS: A REALITY CHECK

As your dog matures, remember what they were bred to do:

RETRIEVERS will want to carry things, may be mouthy, and need
jobs. Use fetch, carry games, and scent work.

HERDING DOGS will try to control movement, may nip at heels,
and need mental challenges. Try obedience, agility, or puzzle
work.

TERRIERS will chase, dig, and shake toys "to death." They
need outlets for their prey drive. Flirt poles and tug work well.

HOUNDS will follow their nose everywhere. Recall may be harder.
Use long lines and scent games.

GUARDIAN BREEDS will naturally be suspicious of strangers. Early
socialisation is essential. They need clear leadership.

WORKING DOGS were bred for jobs. Without work, they create their
own—usually not what you'd choose.

TOY BREEDS are still dogs. Don't carry them everywhere. They
need to walk, explore, and be treated like dogs.

Know your breed. Meet their needs. Don't fight their nature—
work with it.

                    ─────────────────────────────
                           CHAPTER 20

              Health, Grooming, and Lifelong Care
                    ─────────────────────────────

Training is only one part of caring for your dog.

Your puppy also depends on you for their physical health: their
coat, their teeth, their nails, their diet. These things may not
feel like training, but they require the same patience, the same
positive approach, and the same willingness to go slowly.

This chapter covers the basics of physical care—and how to make
these experiences calm and stress-free for both of you.

                              ───

                  YOUR PARTNERSHIP WITH A VETERINARIAN

Your veterinarian is one of your most important partners in
raising a healthy dog.

Choose a vet before you need one urgently:

     •  Ask friends, neighbours, and local trainers for
        recommendations
     •  Visit the clinic before an appointment to see how they
        treat animals
     •  Look for a vet who explains things clearly and listens
        to your concerns
     •  Trust your instincts—if something feels off, keep looking

WHAT TO EXPECT:

     •  Your puppy should see a vet shortly after coming home
     •  Expect vaccinations, a general health check, and advice
        on parasite prevention
     •  Annual checkups are standard for healthy dogs
     •  Your vet can also advise on nutrition, behaviour, and
        when to spay or neuter

MAKING VET VISITS EASIER:

Vet visits can be stressful for puppies. You can help by:

     •  Visiting the clinic just to say hello (no procedures)
     •  Bringing treats and rewarding calm behaviour
     •  Staying calm yourself—your energy matters
     •  Practicing handling at home (touching paws, ears, mouth)

A puppy who learns that the vet clinic is a neutral or positive
place will be easier to care for throughout their life.

                              ───

                  HANDLING: GETTING YOUR PUPPY USED TO TOUCH

Throughout your dog's life, they will be touched by vets, groomers,
and you. The earlier you teach them that handling is safe, the
easier everything becomes.

DAILY HANDLING PRACTICE:

During calm moments, gently touch and handle different parts of
your puppy's body:

     •  Paws and toes (for nail trims)
     •  Ears (for cleaning and vet checks)
     •  Mouth and teeth (for brushing)
     •  Tail and hindquarters
     •  Belly (when they're relaxed)

HOW TO DO IT:

     1. Start when your puppy is already calm—after a nap or
        exercise
     2. Touch briefly, then reward with a treat
     3. Gradually increase duration and pressure
     4. Stop before your puppy becomes uncomfortable
     5. Pair handling with good things: treats, praise, calm voice

If your puppy pulls away or seems stressed, you've gone too fast.
Back up and make it easier next time.

The goal is not just tolerance—it's comfort. A dog who relaxes
during handling is a dog who trusts you.

                              ───

                  NAIL TRIMMING

Long nails can cause discomfort, affect your dog's gait, and even
lead to injury. Regular nail care is essential.

SIGNS YOUR DOG'S NAILS ARE TOO LONG:

     •  You hear clicking on hard floors
     •  Nails touch the ground when standing
     •  Nails curve or grow sideways

GETTING YOUR PUPPY USED TO NAIL TRIMS:

This is a process. Do not rush.

     1. Let your puppy sniff the nail clipper. Reward.
     2. Touch the clipper to their paw. Reward.
     3. Touch the clipper to a nail. Reward.
     4. Clip just the very tip of one nail. Reward generously.
     5. Do one or two nails per session at first.

TIPS:

     •  Use sharp, properly sized clippers
     •  Avoid the "quick" (the blood vessel inside the nail)
     •  If you're nervous, ask your vet or groomer to show you
     •  A nail grinder (Dremel-style) is an alternative some
        dogs tolerate better

If you accidentally cut the quick, stay calm. Apply styptic powder
and move on. Your reaction matters—panicking teaches your dog that
nail trims are scary.

                              ───

                  TEETH AND DENTAL CARE

Dental health is often overlooked, but it matters. Periodontal
disease is common in dogs and can lead to serious health problems
if left untreated.

THE BASICS:

     •  Ideally, brush your dog's teeth daily
     •  Use a dog-specific toothbrush or finger brush
     •  Never use human toothpaste—it's harmful to dogs
     •  Dog toothpaste comes in flavours like chicken or peanut
        butter

INTRODUCING TOOTH BRUSHING:

     1. Let your puppy taste the toothpaste. Reward.
     2. Touch the brush to their lips. Reward.
     3. Gently brush one or two teeth. Reward.
     4. Gradually increase the number of teeth and duration.

OTHER DENTAL SUPPORT:

     •  Dental chews can help reduce plaque
     •  Some foods are designed to support dental health
     •  Your vet should check your dog's teeth at annual visits

Signs of dental problems include bad breath, red gums, difficulty
eating, and pawing at the mouth. See your vet if you notice these.

                              ───

                  EAR CARE

Some dogs need regular ear cleaning; others rarely do. It depends
on breed, ear shape, and activity level.

WHEN TO CLEAN:

     •  Weekly checks are a good habit
     •  Clean when you see visible dirt or buildup
     •  After swimming or baths

HOW TO CLEAN:

     •  Use a vet-recommended ear cleaning solution
     •  Apply to a cotton ball (never insert anything deep into
        the ear canal)
     •  Gently wipe the outer ear and visible folds
     •  Reward calm behaviour throughout

SIGNS OF EAR PROBLEMS:

     •  Redness or swelling
     •  Unusual odor
     •  Excessive scratching at the ears
     •  Head shaking or tilting
     •  Discharge or crusting

If you notice these signs, see your vet. Ear infections are
common and treatable, but they need proper diagnosis.

                              ───

                  COAT CARE AND BATHING

Different coats have different needs. Some dogs require daily
brushing; others need very little.

BRUSHING:

     •  Prevents matting and removes loose fur
     •  Distributes natural oils for a healthy coat
     •  Gives you a chance to check for lumps, bumps, or parasites
     •  Can be a bonding experience when done gently

Choose a brush appropriate for your dog's coat type. When in doubt,
ask your vet or groomer.

BATHING:

     •  Most dogs don't need frequent baths—once a month is often
        enough
     •  Use a mild, dog-specific shampoo
     •  Avoid getting water or soap in eyes, ears, or nose
     •  Rinse thoroughly—leftover shampoo can irritate skin
     •  Dry your dog well, especially in cold weather

If your dog's skin becomes dry, flaky, or irritated, you may be
bathing too often or using the wrong shampoo. Talk to your vet.

INTRODUCING BATHS:

     •  Start with shallow water and lots of treats
     •  Keep early sessions short and positive
     •  Let your puppy stand on a non-slip surface
     •  Stay calm—your energy affects theirs

                              ───

                  WORKING WITH A PROFESSIONAL GROOMER

For some breeds, professional grooming is essential. Even for
others, a groomer can help with things like nail trims, ear
cleaning, and coat maintenance.

FINDING A GOOD GROOMER:

     •  Ask for recommendations from your vet, trainer, or
        dog-owning friends
     •  Visit the facility before booking
     •  Ask how they handle nervous or reactive dogs
     •  Make sure dogs are never left unattended in drying cages

RED FLAGS:

     •  Unwillingness to let you see the grooming area
     •  Rushing you or dismissing your concerns
     •  Dogs looking stressed or fearful
     •  Poor hygiene or chaotic environment

A good groomer will take the time to make your dog comfortable
and will communicate with you about any concerns.

                              ───

                  NUTRITION BASICS

What your dog eats affects their energy, coat, digestion, and
overall health.

CHOOSING A FOOD:

     •  Look for high-quality protein as the first ingredient
     •  Avoid foods with vague ingredients like "meat by-products"
     •  Choose a food appropriate for your dog's life stage
        (puppy, adult, senior)
     •  Work with your vet to find the best option

HOW MUCH TO FEED:

     •  Start with the guidelines on the food packaging
     •  Adjust based on your dog's weight and activity level
     •  You should be able to feel your dog's ribs but not see them
     •  Your vet can help you determine the right amount

TREATS:

     •  Keep training treats small—pea-sized or smaller
     •  Treats should make up no more than 10% of daily calories
     •  Adjust meal portions if you're training heavily

FOODS TO AVOID:

Some human foods are toxic to dogs:

     •  Chocolate
     •  Grapes and raisins
     •  Onions and garlic
     •  Xylitol (artificial sweetener)
     •  Alcohol
     •  Caffeine
     •  Macadamia nuts

When in doubt, don't share. Stick to dog-safe treats.

                              ───

                  SPAYING AND NEUTERING

Spaying (for females) and neutering (for males) are surgical
procedures that prevent reproduction.

WHY IT MATTERS:

     •  Helps reduce pet overpopulation
     •  May reduce certain health risks
     •  Can reduce some unwanted behaviours (though training is
        still essential)

WHEN TO DO IT:

     •  Traditional recommendation: before 6 months of age
     •  Some vets now recommend waiting longer for large breeds
     •  Discuss timing with your vet based on your dog's breed,
        size, and health

COMMON MYTHS:

     •  "My dog will get fat." No—weight gain comes from
        overfeeding and under-exercising, not the surgery itself.
     •  "My male dog will lose his personality." Dogs don't have
        ego about these things. They adapt just fine.
     •  "My female should have one litter first." There's no
        health benefit to this.

Talk to your vet. Make a decision based on facts, not myths.

                              ───

                  HEALTH IS PART OF TRAINING

Everything in this chapter requires the same skills you've been
building throughout this book:

     •  Patience
     •  Positive associations
     •  Calm energy
     •  Going at your dog's pace
     •  Celebrating small wins

A dog who trusts you during training will also trust you during
nail trims, baths, and vet visits.

It's all connected.

                              ───


                    ─────────────────────────────
                         CLOSING THOUGHTS
                    ─────────────────────────────

Raising a puppy is intense, emotional, and sometimes exhausting.

It is also one of the most rewarding experiences you can have
with an animal.

You do not need to be perfect.

You just need to be fair, patient, and willing to learn alongside
your dog.

If you take nothing else from this book, remember this:

     Your dog is always trying their best
     with the skills they have right now.

     Your job is to help them grow those skills,
     one calm day at a time.


                              ───

                  LOOKING FORWARD

The journey doesn't end when puppyhood does.

At six months, you'll start seeing the dog they're becoming.
At one year, the wild puppy energy begins to settle.
At two years, most dogs reach emotional maturity.
At three years and beyond, you'll have a true companion who
knows you as well as you know them.

Every difficult night, every frustrating setback, every moment
when you wondered "Why did I do this?"—they all become part of
the story you'll tell years from now, when this wild creature
is sleeping peacefully at your feet.

Training changes as they grow. You'll move from managing chaos
to refining skills. From teaching basics to enjoying adventures.
From survival mode to genuine partnership.

Some days will still be hard. Adolescence will test you. Life
changes will disrupt routines. But by then, you'll have built
something that sustains you both: trust.


                              ───

                  THE GIFT OF GETTING IT RIGHT

When you train with patience instead of punishment...
When you set clear boundaries with calm consistency...
When you meet their needs before expecting obedience...
When you see the world through their eyes, not just yours...

You don't just get a well-behaved dog.

You get a dog who wants to be with you. A dog who looks at you
with genuine trust. A dog who understands, on some level we
can't fully articulate, that you are their person.

That's not something you can force. It's something you build.
And everything in this book has been pointing you toward it.


                              ───

                  A FINAL WORD

Training is not about dominance.
Training is not about control.
Training is about communication.

Two species, separated by language and evolution, learning to
live together. To understand each other. To keep each other
safe. To make each other's lives better.

That's remarkable.

You're doing remarkable work.

Keep going.

                    ─────────────────────────────
                       QUICK REFERENCE GUIDE
                    ─────────────────────────────

                  POTTY TRAINING AT A GLANCE

     •  Take out immediately after: waking, eating, playing
     •  Every 30–60 minutes when awake
     •  Same spot every time
     •  Praise calmly when successful
     •  Clean accidents with enzyme cleaner
     •  Supervision or confinement—no loose puppies

                              ───

                  BASIC CUES SUMMARY

     SIT:      Lure up and back → bottom goes down → "Yes"
     DOWN:     From sit, lure to floor → forward → "Yes"
     STAY:     Build duration, then distance, then distraction
     COME:     Excited voice → reward generously → never punish
     LEAVE IT: Close fist → wait for look away → reward from
               other hand
     LOOK:     Treat to eyes → "Yes" when they look at you

                              ───

                  COMMON PROBLEMS QUICK FIXES

     BITING:     Freeze → redirect to toy → resume when calm
     JUMPING:    Turn away → ask for sit → reward four on floor
     BARKING:    Wait for pause → "Yes" → reward quiet
     PULLING:    Stop walking → resume when leash loose
     CHEWING:    Manage environment → provide legal outlets

                              ───

                  DAILY RHYTHM REMINDER

     Wake → Potty → Exercise/Play → Train → Eat → Settle → Sleep
     (Repeat throughout the day)

                              ───

                  SIGNS YOUR PUPPY NEEDS A NAP

     •  Increased biting
     •  Zooming/hyperactivity
     •  Ignoring known cues
     •  Excessive barking

     Solution: Crate + quiet = reset

                              ───

                  FEAR PERIOD AGES

     •  First: 8–12 weeks
     •  Second: 6–14 months

     Response: Extra positive experiences, never force, create
     distance if scared

                              ───

                  THE RIGHT ORDER

     1. EXERCISE first (drain energy)
     2. DISCIPLINE second (training, structure, rules)
     3. AFFECTION last (reward the calm state)

     Most people do this backward. Flip the order for better results.

                              ───

                  RULES, BOUNDARIES, LIMITATIONS

     RULES:       What your dog must always/never do
     BOUNDARIES:  Where your dog can/cannot go
     LIMITATIONS: How long or how much (you decide when it ends)

     Clear structure = calm dog. Ambiguity = anxious dog.

                              ───

                  CALM-ASSERTIVE ENERGY

     •  Relaxed but confident
     •  Not angry, rushed, or nervous
     •  Your dog reads your energy before your words

     If you're stressed, your dog feels it. Breathe first.

                              ───

                  NO TOUCH, NO TALK, NO EYE CONTACT

     When meeting a new dog (or when guests arrive):

     •  Ignore the dog at first
     •  Let them sniff and investigate
     •  Wait for calm before acknowledging
     •  Pet chest/side first, not head

     This respects how dogs naturally greet.

                              ───

                  THE FULFILLMENT FORMULA

     EXERCISE + STRUCTURE + AFFECTION = BALANCED DOG

     If behaviour problems persist, ask:
     "Is my dog truly fulfilled?"

                              ───

                  HEALTH & GROOMING AT A GLANCE

     NAILS:    Click on floor = too long. Trim tips only.
               Desensitize slowly with treats.

     TEETH:    Brush daily with dog toothpaste.
               Bad breath or red gums = see vet.

     EARS:     Check weekly. Clean visible areas only.
               Odor, redness, or scratching = see vet.

     BATHS:    Monthly is usually enough.
               Use dog shampoo. Rinse thoroughly.

     VET:      Annual checkups. Build positive associations
               with the clinic before emergencies.

     TOXIC FOODS: Chocolate, grapes, onions, xylitol, alcohol.

                        ABOUT THE AUTHOR
                    ─────────────────────────────

I grew up surrounded by dogs and animals in Italy, where I
first discovered a truth that would shape my entire approach
to training: animals read energy.

From an early age, I observed how a calm, confident presence
could transform an animal's behaviour—how patience and clear
intention spoke louder than words ever could. This understanding,
paired with years of studying the work of modern dog trainers,
became the foundation of my philosophy.

For me, two things matter most: energy and discipline.

Energy because dogs sense what we feel before we speak. A calm,
assertive presence communicates more than any command. And
discipline—not punishment, but structure. Clear rules, consistent
boundaries, and redirection toward the behaviours we want to see.
This is how dogs learn to make good choices on their own.

Now based in Berlin, I've dedicated myself to helping new puppy
owners in my community navigate the challenges of raising a
well-behaved dog. What started as informal guidance for neighbours
quickly grew into something bigger—a passion for teaching that
couldn't stay small.

This book was inspired by Kayra, my Rhodesian Ridgeback. The
Ridgeback is an incredibly smart and challenging breed—and
Kayra taught me as much as I taught her. She showed me the
importance of my own discipline, the power of consistency, and
why positive reinforcement matters so deeply for dogs. Through
her, I learned that training is never just about the dog. It's
about who we become in the process.

I believe that anyone can raise a confident, balanced dog—not
through force or fear, but through structure, calm energy, and
genuine connection.
